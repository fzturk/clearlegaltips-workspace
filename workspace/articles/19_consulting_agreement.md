## What Is a Consulting Agreement?

A consulting agreement is a legally binding contract between a consultant (an individual or firm providing expert advice and services) and a client (the individual or company receiving those services). It defines the scope of consulting engagement, compensation terms, intellectual property rights, confidentiality obligations, and the terms under which either party can end the relationship.

**Consulting agreement vs. service agreement:** While similar, a consulting agreement typically involves advisory, strategic, or expert knowledge work — not deliverable-based project work. A consultant advises; a service provider executes. In practice, many engagements blend both. Our template handles this with a flexible SOW exhibit structure.

**Consulting agreement vs. employment contract:** The consulting agreement explicitly establishes the consultant as an independent contractor. This distinction has major tax, liability, and benefit implications for both parties.

### Section 6: Independent Contractor Status

**Consultant is NOT an employee:**
- Controls methods and schedule of work
- Uses own equipment and office
- May work for other clients simultaneously
- Responsible for own taxes (self-employment, estimated quarterly)
- Client issues Form 1099-NEC (not W-2)
- No employee benefits: no health insurance, PTO, retirement, workers' comp
- Consultant maintains own business insurance

**IRS classification safeguards:**
- Behavioral control: Consultant determines HOW to perform work
- Financial control: Consultant sets rates, bears business expenses, can profit or lose
- Relationship: Temporary engagement, not indefinite employment

### Section 7: Intellectual Property

**Option A: Client Owns All Work Product**
All work product, deliverables, reports, analysis, and recommendations created under this agreement are "work made for hire" and owned exclusively by Client.

**Option B: Consultant Retains Ownership, Client Gets License**
Consultant retains ownership of all work product. Client receives a [perpetual/limited] [exclusive/non-exclusive] license to use deliverables for [SPECIFIED PURPOSES].

**Option C: Joint Ownership**
Work product jointly owned. Each party may use without the other's consent, subject to confidentiality.

**Pre-existing IP:** Consultant's pre-existing tools, methodologies, frameworks, and templates remain Consultant's property. Client receives a non-exclusive license to use them within the deliverables.

### Section 8: Confidentiality

**Consultant obligations:**
- Protect all Client confidential information (business plans, financial data, trade secrets, customer lists, proprietary processes)
- Use only for purposes of the engagement
- Do not disclose to third parties without written consent
- Return or destroy all confidential materials upon termination

**Client obligations:**
- Protect Consultant's proprietary methodologies and pricing
- Do not share Consultant's deliverables with competitors

**Duration:** [2/3/5] years after termination of agreement
**Exclusions:** Public information, prior knowledge, independently developed, legally compelled

### Section 9: Non-Compete

During the engagement and for [6/12] months after termination:
- Consultant shall not provide competing services to [SPECIFIC COMPETITORS / companies in CLIENT'S INDUSTRY]
- Geographic scope: [ ] Nationwide / [ ] Within [REGION/STATE]
- Does not restrict Consultant from serving non-competing clients in the same industry

**Reasonableness requirement:** Non-competes must be reasonable in scope, duration, and geography to be enforceable. Overly broad restrictions are void in many states. California generally prohibits all non-competes for independent contractors.

### Section 10: Non-Solicitation

- Consultant shall not solicit Client's employees or contractors
- Consultant shall not solicit Client's customers/clients for competing services
- Client shall not solicit Consultant's employees or subcontractors
- "Solicit" does not include responding to public job postings or general advertising

### Section 11: Representations and Warranties

**Consultant warrants:**
- Qualified and experienced to perform the services
- Work will be performed in a professional manner consistent with industry standards
- Has authority to enter this agreement and no conflicting obligations
- Work will not infringe third-party intellectual property

**Limitation of liability:**
- Consultant's maximum liability: total fees paid under this agreement in the prior [6/12] months
- Neither party liable for indirect, consequential, or lost-profit damages
- Client responsible for business decisions made using Consultant's advice

### Section 12: Insurance

## How to Fill Out Your Consulting Agreement (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Define the Engagement Type

- **Advisory/strategic:** Ongoing counsel on business decisions — hourly or retainer works best
- **Project-based:** Defined deliverables with clear endpoints — fixed fee or project SOW
- **Fractional executive:** Part-time leadership role — monthly retainer with defined hours/responsibilities

### Step 2: Choose the Right Compensation Structure

- **Hourly** if: Scope is uncertain, engagement is open-ended, or value is tied to time invested
- **Retainer** if: Client wants guaranteed availability, relationship is ongoing, work is predictable monthly
- **Project fee** if: Scope is clearly defined, deliverables are concrete, and timeline is fixed

### Step 3: Address IP Before Starting Work

Who owns the strategic framework you develop? The market analysis? The recommended process changes? Decide this NOW. Most consultants retain ownership of their methodologies while transferring deliverables.

### Step 4: Set Clear Boundaries on Scope

Without scope boundaries, a "strategy consultation" becomes "help me with everything." Define what's included and what triggers a new SOW or additional fees.

### Step 5: Protect Your Methodology

Your consulting framework, processes, and analytical tools are your competitive advantage. The pre-existing IP clause ensures you can use your own methodology with future clients even after this engagement ends.

### Step 6: Execute and Maintain

- Both parties sign the master agreement
- Execute SOW #1 for the first project/phase
- Each party keeps copies
- Review annually for ongoing engagements
- Create new SOWs for new projects/phases

## Common Consulting Agreement Mistakes

### Mistake 1: No Scope Boundaries
"Consulting on business strategy" means everything and nothing. Define specific deliverables, meeting frequency, response time expectations, and what triggers out-of-scope billing.

### Mistake 2: Hourly Without a Cap
Clients fear unlimited hourly bills. Set a monthly hour estimate or a not-to-exceed amount that requires written approval to surpass. This builds trust and prevents billing disputes.

### Mistake 3: No SOW Structure for Evolving Engagements
Starting a consulting relationship with one agreement and never updating the scope as work evolves creates ambiguity about what's billable. Use SOWs for each phase.

### Mistake 4: Giving Away Your Methodology
If your agreement says "all work product belongs to Client" without carving out pre-existing IP, you may lose the right to use your own consulting frameworks with future clients.

### Mistake 5: No Termination Provisions
Engagements that drag on without clear termination rights trap both parties. Include convenience termination with reasonable notice and payment for completed work.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between a consulting agreement and an independent contractor agreement?

They're closely related, but a consulting agreement specifically covers expert advisory services — strategy, analysis, recommendations. An independent contractor agreement is broader, covering any non-employee work (construction, design, writing, etc.). A consulting agreement often includes provisions for retainers, SOWs, and methodology protection that aren't typical in general contractor agreements.

### Should I use a consulting agreement or a service agreement?

If you're providing expert advice and strategic guidance: consulting agreement. If you're producing specific deliverables (designs, code, content): service agreement. Many engagements blend both — use whichever template better fits the primary nature of your work, or use the consulting agreement with a detailed SOW for deliverables.

### Can a consulting agreement protect me from client misuse of my advice?

Yes — the limitation of liability clause is critical. Your agreement should state that the client is responsible for business decisions made using your advice, and that your liability is capped at fees paid. This protects you from claims that your strategic recommendation didn't produce the expected results.

### How do I handle scope creep in a consulting engagement?

Reference your agreement: additional work outside the current SOW requires a new SOW or written approval with associated fees. Track your hours carefully. When the client asks for something beyond scope, respond with: "I'm happy to take that on. Let me draft a change order with the estimated hours and cost."

### Is a retainer refundable if the client doesn't use all the hours?

Per your agreement terms — our template lets you choose. Most consultants make retainers non-refundable (the client pays for availability, not just work performed). Others allow rollover of unused hours (with a cap). Whatever you decide, make it explicit in the agreement.
