## What Is a Promissory Note?

A Promissory Note is a written, legally binding promise by one party (the borrower/maker) to pay a specific sum of money to another party (the lender/payee) under agreed-upon terms. It documents the loan amount, interest rate, repayment schedule, and consequences of default.

Unlike a casual IOU (which merely acknowledges a debt exists), a Promissory Note specifies exactly HOW and WHEN the debt will be repaid, what interest applies, and what happens if the borrower doesn't pay. This specificity makes it enforceable in court.

**The legal hierarchy of debt documentation:**

| Document | Enforceability | Detail Level | Best For |
|----------|---------------|-------------|----------|
| Verbal promise | Very weak — hard to prove | None | Never rely on this |
| IOU | Weak — acknowledges debt only | Minimal | Informal small amounts |
| **Promissory Note** | **Strong — fully enforceable** | **Complete terms** | **All loans** |
| Loan Agreement | Strongest — bilateral contract | Extensive | Complex/large loans |

**Key distinction:** A Promissory Note is a unilateral promise (only the borrower signs, promising to pay). A Loan Agreement is a bilateral contract (both parties sign, with the lender also making commitments like disbursement terms and conditions). For most personal and small business loans, a Promissory Note is sufficient.

## When Do You Need a Promissory Note? (9 Scenarios)

### Personal Loans

1. **Lending money to family or friends** — The #1 reason relationships break over money: no documentation. A Promissory Note keeps the relationship healthy by setting clear expectations both parties agreed to.

2. **Receiving a personal loan from someone** — Protect yourself from future claims that the amount was different, the terms were different, or the "loan" was actually a gift (which could have tax implications).

3. **Settling a debt in installments** — When you owe someone money and can't pay all at once, a Promissory Note documents the agreed payment plan.

### Business & Real Estate

4. **Seller financing (owner carry-back)** — When a business or property seller agrees to accept payments over time instead of requiring full upfront payment.

5. **Business loans between partners or members** — When an LLC member loans money to the company (or vice versa), documentation distinguishes it from a capital contribution.

6. **Down payment loans** — When a family member helps with a down payment, mortgage lenders want to see whether it's a gift or a loan. A Promissory Note clarifies.

7. **Employee loans or advances** — When an employer advances pay or loans funds to an employee.

### Specific Transactions

8. **Vehicle seller financing** — When selling a car and the buyer pays in installments. The Promissory Note is paired with a lien on the title.

9. **Security deposit alternatives** — Some rental situations use Promissory Notes as alternatives to cash security deposits.

## Secured vs. Unsecured: Which Do You Need?

This is the most important decision when creating your Promissory Note:

### Unsecured Promissory Note

**No collateral backs the loan.** The lender relies solely on the borrower's promise and creditworthiness.

**When to use:**
- Small personal loans ($500-$5,000)
- Loans to trusted family/friends
- Situations where the borrower has no assets to pledge
- Short-term loans you expect to be repaid quickly

**Risk:** If the borrower doesn't pay, your only remedy is to sue for the amount owed. If the borrower has no assets, a court judgment may be uncollectible.

**Legal action available:** Breach of contract lawsuit, wage garnishment (if judgment obtained), credit reporting.

### Secured Promissory Note

**Specific property (collateral) backs the loan.** If the borrower defaults, the lender can seize the collateral.

**When to use:**
- Large loans ($5,000+)
- Vehicle seller financing (car is collateral)
- Real estate loans (property is collateral via deed of trust/mortgage)
- Business equipment loans
- Any situation where you want maximum protection

**Types of collateral:**
- **Real property** — Secured by a deed of trust or mortgage (recorded with the county)
- **Vehicles** — Secured by a lien on the title (filed with DMV)
- **Business equipment** — Secured by a UCC-1 financing statement (filed with Secretary of State)
- **Personal property** — Secured by a security agreement
- **Financial accounts** — Secured by a pledge of deposits or investments

**To properly secure a loan, you need BOTH:**
1. The Promissory Note (the promise to pay)
2. A Security Agreement (the pledge of collateral)

For vehicles: file a lien on the title. For real estate: record a deed of trust. For business assets: file a UCC-1. Our template includes security agreement language, but complex secured loans may need additional documents.

## Understanding Repayment Structures (4 Options in Our Template)

### Option A: Installment Payments (Most Common)

Equal payments of principal + interest over a fixed period. Like a mortgage or car payment.

**Example:** $10,000 loan at 6% interest, 24 monthly payments = $443.21/month

**Best for:** Most personal and business loans where regular income allows consistent payments.

**Our template includes:** A full amortization schedule showing how each payment splits between principal and interest.

### Option B: Lump Sum (Balloon Payment)

The entire principal (plus accumulated interest) is due on a single future date. No payments are required before that date.

**Example:** $5,000 loan at 5% interest, due in full on December 31, 2027 = $5,250 due at maturity.

**Best for:** Bridge loans, loans where the borrower expects a lump sum (tax refund, bonus, property sale), short-term financing.

**Risk:** If the borrower can't pay the balloon, the entire amount is immediately in default. Consider including a conversion clause allowing switch to installments.

### Option C: Interest-Only with Balloon

Borrower pays only interest each month. The full principal is due at the end of the term.

**Example:** $20,000 loan at 7% interest, 12 months of interest-only = $116.67/month for 12 months, then $20,000 principal due at the end.

**Best for:** Real estate investors (cash flow during renovation), business seasonal income, situations where the borrower needs low monthly payments now but expects future ability to pay in full.

### Option D: On Demand (Demand Note)

No fixed schedule. The full amount is due whenever the lender demands payment (usually with a notice period of 10-30 days).

**Example:** $3,000 loan, payable upon 15 days' written demand from lender.

**Best for:** Informal family loans, open-ended lending relationships, lines of credit.

**Risk for borrower:** The lender can demand full payment at any time. Risk for lender: no structured payments means you might never see the money.

## How to Fill Out Your Promissory Note (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Determine the Loan Structure

Before filling anything in, agree on:
- How much is being borrowed?
- What interest rate is fair? (Check AFR — see below)
- How will it be repaid? (installments, lump sum, etc.)
- Over what timeframe?
- Is there collateral?

### Step 2: Check the Applicable Federal Rate (AFR)

The IRS publishes monthly Applicable Federal Rates. If you charge interest BELOW the AFR on a loan over $10,000, the IRS imputes interest — meaning the lender owes tax on interest they didn't actually receive.

**May 2026 AFR ranges (check IRS.gov for current rates):**
- Short-term (3 years or less): ~4.5-5.5%
- Mid-term (3-9 years): ~4.0-5.0%
- Long-term (over 9 years): ~4.5-5.5%

**Rule of thumb:** Set your interest rate at or above the AFR to avoid IRS complications.

### Step 3: Fill in Party Information

- Full legal names (as they appear on government ID)
- Current addresses
- If a business is the borrower, use the entity's legal name

### Step 4: Enter Financial Terms

- Principal amount in both numbers and words: "$10,000 (Ten Thousand Dollars)"
- Annual interest rate
- Monthly payment amount (use an amortization calculator)
- First payment date and maturity date
- Late fee amount and grace period

### Step 5: Choose Secured or Unsecured

If secured:
- Describe collateral with specificity (VIN for vehicles, address for real estate, serial numbers for equipment)
- Determine whether you'll file a lien, deed of trust, or UCC-1
- Include insurance requirements for the collateral

### Step 6: Add Co-Signer if Applicable

If the borrower's ability to repay is uncertain, a co-signer provides backup. The co-signer must:
- Be present at signing
- Sign separately in the guarantor section
- Understand they're equally liable for the full amount

### Step 7: Sign and Secure

- Borrower signs (and co-signer, if applicable)
- The lender does NOT typically sign a Promissory Note (it's a one-way promise)
- Notarization recommended for amounts over $5,000
- If secured: file appropriate lien documents immediately
- Each party receives a copy; lender holds the original

## Common Promissory Note Mistakes

### Mistake 1: No Written Note at All

The most common mistake: trusting a verbal promise. Courts require clear evidence of loan terms. Without a written note, proving the loan existed — let alone the terms — is extremely difficult.

### Mistake 2: Charging Interest Above Legal Limits

Usury violations can void the entire loan, forfeit all interest, and even create criminal liability. Always check your state's maximum rate before setting your interest.

### Mistake 3: No Acceleration Clause

Without acceleration, if the borrower stops paying, you can only sue for each missed payment individually (not the entire remaining balance). Always include an acceleration clause.

### Mistake 4: Failing to File the Security Interest

Having a "secured" Promissory Note means nothing if you don't perfect your security interest. File the lien on the vehicle title, record the deed of trust, or file the UCC-1. Otherwise, you're effectively unsecured.

### Mistake 5: Below-AFR Interest on Family Loans Over $10,000

The IRS will impute interest at the AFR even if you charged less. This means the lender owes income tax on phantom interest income. Either charge at least the AFR, or keep the loan under $10,000.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a Promissory Note legally binding without a notary?

Yes. A Promissory Note is a contract, and contracts are valid with just signatures (in most cases). Notarization adds evidentiary weight — it proves the borrower actually signed and wasn't forged — but it's not legally required in most states for the note to be enforceable.

### Can I charge interest on a personal loan?

Yes. You can charge any rate up to your state's usury limit. However, for loans over $10,000, the IRS requires you to charge at least the Applicable Federal Rate (AFR). If you charge less (including 0%), the IRS imputes interest income to the lender anyway.

### What happens if the borrower dies?

The debt becomes an obligation of the borrower's estate. The executor must pay debts from estate assets before distributing to heirs. If the estate has insufficient assets, the debt may be partially or fully uncollectible — unless there's a co-signer or the note is secured by collateral that survives death.

### Can I sell or transfer a Promissory Note?

Yes. Promissory Notes are negotiable instruments (like checks) and can be sold, assigned, or transferred. The new holder steps into the lender's shoes. This is common in real estate (mortgage notes are regularly sold to investors).

### What's the difference between a Promissory Note and an IOU?

An IOU simply acknowledges a debt exists ("I owe you $500"). A Promissory Note goes further: it specifies the repayment schedule, interest rate, consequences of default, and other enforceable terms. Courts strongly prefer Promissory Notes because they contain all terms needed for enforcement.

### How do I enforce a Promissory Note if the borrower defaults?

Steps: (1) Send written notice of default with cure period, (2) If unsecured: file a civil lawsuit for the balance, (3) If secured: exercise your right to seize collateral (following state-specific procedures), (4) Obtain a court judgment, (5) Collect through wage garnishment, bank levy, or property lien. For amounts under $5,000-$10,000 (varies by state), small claims court is fastest.
