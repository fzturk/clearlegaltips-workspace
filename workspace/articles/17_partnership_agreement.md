## What Is a Partnership Agreement?

A partnership agreement (also called articles of partnership or partnership contract) is a legally binding document that establishes the terms and conditions of a business relationship between two or more individuals or entities who agree to carry on a business together for profit.

**Without a written agreement**, your state's Uniform Partnership Act (UPA) or Revised Uniform Partnership Act (RUPA) fills in every blank — and those default rules rarely match what partners actually intend.

**Default rules you probably don't want:**
- Equal profit split regardless of unequal contributions
- Every partner has equal management authority (even silent investors)
- Any partner can bind the entire partnership to contracts
- Death of one partner automatically dissolves the partnership
- No non-compete after departure — your partner can open a competing business tomorrow

A written partnership agreement overrides nearly all of these defaults.

## General Partnership vs. Limited Partnership

### General Partnership (GP)

All partners share management responsibilities AND unlimited personal liability.

**Key characteristics:**
- Every partner can bind the partnership
- Every partner is personally liable for ALL partnership debts
- Profits/losses pass through to individual tax returns (Form 1065 → K-1)
- No state filing required to form (but registration recommended)
- Simplest structure — lowest formation cost

**Best for:** Professional firms, small businesses where all partners actively participate, service businesses.

### Limited Partnership (LP)

Has two classes of partners with different rights and liabilities.

**General Partners (GP):**
- Manage the business
- Have unlimited personal liability
- Can bind the partnership

**Limited Partners (LP):**
- Passive investors only
- Liability limited to their investment
- Cannot participate in management (or risk losing liability protection)
- Receive distributions but don't run operations

**Best for:** Real estate investment, venture capital, family businesses where some members invest but don't operate.

| Feature | General Partnership | Limited Partnership |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Formation | No filing required | Must file with state |
| Liability | Unlimited for ALL | Unlimited for GPs; limited for LPs |
| Management | All partners | General partners only |
| Tax treatment | Pass-through (1065) | Pass-through (1065) |
| Cost to form | $0-$100 | $200-$1,000+ |
| Annual filing | Varies by state | Required in most states |
| Transferability | Restricted | LP interests more transferable |
| Dissolution | Partner withdrawal triggers | Survives LP withdrawal |

## When Do You Need a Partnership Agreement?

### Business Operations

1. **Starting a business with a friend or family member** — The #1 source of partnership disputes. Written terms prevent "I thought we agreed..." conversations.

2. **Professional practices** — Law firms, medical practices, accounting firms, architectural firms, consulting groups.

3. **Real estate investment groups** — Multiple investors pooling capital for property acquisition, development, or management.

4. **Retail or restaurant ventures** — One partner provides capital; the other provides operational expertise and daily management.

### Investment Structures

5. **Angel investor groups** — Multiple investors forming a partnership to invest in startups together.

6. **Real estate syndications** — GP manages properties; LPs provide capital and receive returns.

7. **Family investment partnerships** — Structured for estate planning, asset protection, and generational wealth transfer.

### Creative and Professional Collaborations

8. **Joint professional practices** — Two attorneys sharing an office, staff, and clients while splitting revenue.

9. **Agricultural operations** — Family farms or ranches where multiple parties contribute land, labor, and capital.

10. **Franchise operations** — Multiple investors partnering to purchase and operate a franchise.

## How to Fill Out Your Partnership Agreement (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Agree on Business Structure First

Before touching the template, partners must decide:
- General Partnership or Limited Partnership?
- Who's a General Partner vs. Limited Partner (if LP)?
- What's the business purpose and scope?

### Step 2: Value All Contributions Honestly

The biggest source of partnership disputes: unequal valuation of contributions. Cash is easy to value. Services, relationships, and intellectual property are not.

Get agreement on:
- How much is Partner A's "industry expertise" worth vs. Partner B's $100,000 cash?
- If one partner contributes property, get a professional appraisal
- Document EVERYTHING — verbal agreements about value fall apart

### Step 3: Define Roles Clearly

Each partner's day-to-day responsibilities should be explicit. Avoid shared responsibilities for the same function. One partner handles finances; another handles operations; a third handles sales. Overlap creates conflict.

### Step 4: Set Decision-Making Rules Before You Disagree

Define voting thresholds BEFORE any contentious decisions arise:
- What does each partner have authority to do alone?
- What requires majority approval?
- What requires unanimous consent?
- What happens when you're deadlocked?

### Step 5: Plan for the End From the Beginning

Every partnership ends — through success (sale), evolution (someone leaves), or failure (dissolution). Your agreement should cover all three scenarios with specific procedures, timelines, and financial terms.

### Step 6: Execute Properly

- All partners sign the same document
- Notarize (recommended, not always required)
- Each partner keeps an original
- If LP: file Certificate of Limited Partnership with the state
- Review and update annually

## Partnership Agreement vs. LLC Operating Agreement

Many businesses that would have formed partnerships now choose LLCs instead. Key differences:

| Factor | General Partnership | LLC |
|--------|-------------------|-----|
| Personal liability | Unlimited | Limited to investment |
| Formation | Informal (can be oral) | State filing required |
| Tax treatment | Pass-through | Pass-through (default) |
| Management flexibility | Equal by default | Highly customizable |
| Transferability | Restricted | Per operating agreement |
| Annual costs | Low | State fees vary |
| Creditor protection | None | Charging order protection |

**When partnership still makes sense:**
- Professional firms (some states don't allow professional LLCs)
- Short-term joint projects (simpler than forming an LLC)
- Family businesses with estate planning considerations (FLPs)
- States with no LLC statute advantages over partnership

## Common Partnership Agreement Mistakes

### Mistake 1: No Written Agreement
Relying on a handshake means relying on state default rules. When a dispute arises — and it will — each partner remembers the verbal agreement differently. Get it in writing.

### Mistake 2: Equal Split Despite Unequal Contributions
50/50 seems fair until one partner contributes $200,000 and the other contributes "hard work." Ownership should reflect the actual value each partner brings — and that needs honest discussion upfront.

### Mistake 3: No Exit Strategy
Partners who plan for how to start but not how to end create legal nightmares. Your agreement should address voluntary withdrawal, involuntary expulsion, death, disability, and full dissolution.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Deadlock Provisions
Two-partner businesses with 50/50 voting power and no tiebreaker mechanism become paralyzed when partners disagree. Include a mediation/arbitration clause or a "shotgun" buyout provision.

### Mistake 5: No Non-Compete Clause
Without a non-compete, a departing partner can open an identical business across the street, take your clients, and recruit your employees — perfectly legally.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a partnership agreement to start a business with someone?

Legally, no — a partnership can form through verbal agreement or even through conduct (operating a business together). But without a written agreement, state default rules govern your partnership, and those rules rarely match your actual intentions. A written agreement is essential for any serious business relationship.

### Can a partnership agreement be changed after signing?

Yes — with the consent of all partners (or the required vote specified in the agreement). Any amendments should be in writing, signed by all partners, and attached to the original agreement. Some agreements allow amendment by majority vote; others require unanimity.

### What happens if my partner dies without a buyout provision?

Without a buyout provision, the deceased partner's estate becomes your new partner. The estate's executor or the partner's heirs inherit their partnership interest — including management rights in a general partnership. This often creates impossible situations where grieving family members with no business experience are suddenly your co-managers.

### Is a partnership agreement the same as a joint venture agreement?

No. A partnership is an ongoing business entity with broad scope. A joint venture is a temporary arrangement for a specific project with a defined end date. A JV limits authority and liability to the venture scope; a partnership creates broader mutual obligations.

### How much does it cost to have a partnership agreement drafted?

DIY template: $0-$50. Online legal service: $100-$500. Attorney-drafted: $1,500-$5,000 for a simple GP agreement; $3,000-$10,000+ for a limited partnership. The cost scales with complexity — number of partners, capital involved, and special provisions needed.

### Can I have a partnership with an LLC?

Yes — an LLC can be a partner in a partnership. This is common in real estate (an LLC serves as the General Partner, providing liability protection to the individuals behind it). Individuals can also be partners alongside LLCs. The partnership agreement should identify each partner by their legal entity name.
