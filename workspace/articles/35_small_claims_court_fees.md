## What Can You Sue For in Small Claims Court?

### Common Small Claims Cases

| Case Type | Examples |
|-----------|---------|
| Unpaid debts | Loans, invoices, bounced checks |
| Property damage | Car accidents, property destruction |
| Security deposit disputes | Landlord won't return deposit |
| Breach of contract | Services not performed, goods not delivered |
| Defective products | Consumer purchases that failed |
| Auto accidents (property damage) | Damage below the limit |
| Neighbor disputes | Property damage, nuisance |
| Bad checks | NSF checks up to the limit |
| Professional services disputes | Contractor didn't finish, quality issues |

### What You CANNOT Sue For

- Personal injury above the dollar limit
- Defamation (some states)
- Eviction (separate process)
- Divorce or custody
- Tax disputes
- Criminal matters
- Class actions

## How to File a Small Claims Case (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Verify Your Claim Fits
- Is the amount within your state's dollar limit?
- Is the type of claim allowed in small claims court?
- Are you filing in the correct county (usually where defendant lives or incident occurred)?

### Step 2: Send a Demand Letter
Before filing, send the other party a written demand for payment. Many courts require this, and it often resolves the dispute without court.

**The demand letter should include:**
- What is owed and why
- Specific dollar amount demanded
- Deadline to pay (14-30 days)
- Statement that you'll file in court if not paid

### Step 3: File the Claim
- Go to your local courthouse (or file online if available)
- Complete the small claims complaint form
- Pay the filing fee
- Provide the defendant's name and address for service

### Step 4: Serve the Defendant
The court or you must formally notify the defendant:
- Court clerk mails certified notice (most states)
- You hire a process server ($30-$75)
- Sheriff serves the papers ($25-$75)

### Step 5: Prepare for the Hearing
- Organize all evidence (contracts, receipts, photos, texts, emails)
- Prepare a brief, clear explanation of your claim
- Bring witnesses if they help prove your case
- Practice your presentation (you typically get 5-15 minutes)

### Step 6: Attend the Hearing
- Arrive early, dress professionally
- Present your case clearly and concisely
- Show evidence when referenced
- Answer the judge's questions directly
- Remain calm regardless of what the other party says

### Step 7: Collect Your Judgment
Winning doesn't guarantee payment. If the defendant doesn't pay voluntarily:
- Wage garnishment (through the court)
- Bank account levy
- Property lien
- Report to credit bureaus (some jurisdictions)

## Tips for Winning in Small Claims Court

### Document Everything
Written evidence wins cases. Bring:
- Signed contracts or agreements
- Invoices and receipts
- Photos or videos (dated)
- Text messages and emails
- Written correspondence (including your demand letter)
- Witness statements

### Be Concise
Judges hear dozens of cases per day. Present your claim in 5 minutes or less:
1. What happened (briefly)
2. What you're owed (specifically)
3. Your evidence (show it)

### Know the Law
You don't need to cite statutes, but understanding basic contract law or landlord-tenant law strengthens your case. A brief consultation with an attorney before the hearing ($100-$200) can help you prepare.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a lawyer for small claims court?

In most states: no, and some states don't even allow attorneys. Small claims is designed for self-representation. However, if the other party has a lawyer and your state allows it, consider at least a consultation to prepare.

### What if I win but the other party doesn't pay?

The court doesn't automatically collect money for you. You must pursue collection: wage garnishment, bank levy, property lien. Some states have "debtor's examinations" where the judge orders the losing party to disclose assets.

### Can I sue for more than the small claims limit?

You can "waive" the amount above the limit (sue for the maximum and forfeit the rest), or file in regular civil court (higher fees, more complex process, attorney usually needed).

### How long does a small claims case take?

Filing to hearing: typically 30-75 days. The hearing itself: 15-60 minutes. Judgment usually issued the same day or within 1-2 weeks.

### Can a business be sued in small claims court?

Yes — businesses are common defendants. You'll sue the legal entity (LLC name, corporation name). Some states have lower limits for businesses filing as plaintiffs (California: $5,000 for business plaintiffs vs. $10,000 for individuals).
