## What Is a Prenuptial Agreement?

A prenuptial agreement (also called a "prenup," "premarital agreement," or "antenuptial agreement") is a legally binding contract between two people who plan to marry. It defines how assets, debts, income, and support obligations will be handled during the marriage AND in the event of divorce or death.

Without a prenup, your state's divorce and property laws make these decisions for you — and those default rules often produce results neither spouse anticipated or wanted.

**What a prenup CAN do:**
- Define which assets remain separate property (protected from division)
- Specify how jointly-acquired assets will be divided
- Protect one spouse from the other's pre-existing debts
- Establish (or waive) spousal support obligations
- Protect a business from division in divorce
- Define inheritance rights
- Set financial expectations for the marriage (joint accounts, budgeting roles)

**What a prenup CANNOT do:**
- Determine child custody or child support (courts decide based on best interest of the child)
- Include provisions that encourage divorce
- Contain terms that are "unconscionable" (so unfair they shock the conscience)
- Waive rights to necessities (food, shelter, clothing)
- Include non-financial personal terms ("you must cook dinner 3x per week") — courts won't enforce these
- Be used as a tool of coercion or abuse

## Who Needs a Prenuptial Agreement?

The old perception that prenups are only for the wealthy is outdated. In 2026, prenups are common (and recommended) for:

### Financial Protection Scenarios

1. **One or both spouses own a business** — Without a prenup, a business started before marriage could be partially awarded to the non-owner spouse (especially if it grew during the marriage).

2. **Significant pre-marital assets** — A home, inheritance, investment portfolio, or savings you want to keep separate regardless of what happens.

3. **One spouse has significant debt** — Student loans, credit card debt, or prior business debts. A prenup can prevent one spouse from becoming liable for the other's pre-existing obligations.

4. **Second marriage (especially with children from prior relationships)** — Protect assets intended for children from your first marriage while still providing for your new spouse.

5. **Expected inheritance** — If you're likely to receive a significant inheritance, a prenup can definitively classify it as separate property.

6. **Significant income disparity** — When one spouse earns substantially more, a prenup clarifies expectations about support obligations and asset accumulation.

### Practical Clarity Scenarios

7. **Both spouses want financial independence** — Some couples want completely separate finances with clear rules about shared expenses.

8. **Protecting a professional practice** — Doctors, lawyers, dentists, and other professionals may want to ensure their practice isn't divided.

9. **Real estate investors** — Properties acquired before marriage (or with separate funds during marriage) need clear ownership rules.

10. **Entrepreneurs with startup equity** — Stock options, vesting schedules, and future company value are complex assets that benefit from clear classification.

## What Makes a Prenup Enforceable (5 Essential Requirements)

Courts can and DO invalidate prenuptial agreements. To ensure yours holds up:

### 1. Voluntary Execution (No Coercion)

Both parties must sign voluntarily. Courts look at:
- **Timing:** Was the prenup presented the night before the wedding? (Bad — suggests pressure)
- **Pressure:** Did one party threaten to cancel the wedding if the other didn't sign?
- **Opportunity:** Did both parties have adequate time to review? (Recommended: 30+ days before wedding)

**Best practice:** Present the prenup at least 30-60 days before the wedding. This gives both parties time to review, negotiate, and consult attorneys without time pressure.

### 2. Full Financial Disclosure

Both parties must completely disclose their financial situation:
- All assets (with values)
- All debts (with balances)
- Income (salary, investments, business income)
- Expected inheritances (if known)

**Why this matters:** A prenup based on hidden assets is voidable. If your spouse later discovers you had a $500,000 account you didn't disclose, the entire agreement could be thrown out.

Our template includes Schedule A and Schedule B — complete financial disclosure forms for each party.

### 3. Independent Legal Counsel

While not universally required, independent counsel for EACH party dramatically increases enforceability:
- Each party should have their OWN attorney (not the same one)
- Each attorney reviews the agreement with their client
- Each attorney signs acknowledging their representation

**States requiring independent counsel:**
- California: Requires independent counsel OR a 7-day waiting period with written waiver
- Some states: Won't enforce without independent counsel for BOTH parties

### 4. Fairness (Not Unconscionable)

The agreement cannot be so one-sided that it "shocks the conscience." Courts evaluate fairness:
- **At execution:** Was it fair when signed?
- **At enforcement:** Is it still fair at the time of divorce? (Some courts use this standard)

An agreement that leaves one spouse destitute while the other walks away with millions is likely unconscionable — even if signed voluntarily.

### 5. Proper Execution

- Written (oral prenups are never enforceable)
- Signed by both parties
- Notarized (recommended; required in some states)
- Witnessed (required in some states)
- Before the marriage (signed after marriage = postnuptial agreement, different rules)

### Section 1: Recitals (Background)

Establishes the framework:
- Both parties intend to marry
- Both have made full financial disclosure
- Both have had opportunity for independent counsel

### Section 2: Definitions

- **Separate Property:** Owned before marriage + inheritances + gifts from third parties + income on separate property (if designated)
- **Marital Property:** Acquired during marriage with marital funds
- **Commingled Property:** Separate property mixed with marital property to the extent it's untraceable

### Section 3: Separate Property Provisions

Each party's pre-marital assets remain theirs regardless of:
- Appreciation during marriage (passive appreciation stays separate)
- Length of marriage
- Other spouse's contributions to maintenance or improvement (unless agreed otherwise)

Three options:

## How to Create Your Prenuptial Agreement (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Start the Conversation Early (3-6 Months Before Wedding)

Frame it positively: "I want us to discuss finances openly and make sure we're on the same page about our financial future." NOT: "I want to protect MY assets from YOU."

### Step 2: Complete Financial Disclosure (Both Parties)

Gather for each person:
- Bank account statements (all accounts)
- Investment account statements
- Real estate valuations (recent appraisals or comparable sales)
- Business valuations (if applicable)
- Retirement account balances
- Debt summary (student loans, credit cards, mortgages, personal loans)
- Income documentation (recent tax returns, pay stubs)

### Step 3: Discuss and Agree on Key Terms

Talk about:
- What assets do you each want to protect as separate?
- How will you handle joint finances during marriage?
- What's fair regarding spousal support if the marriage ends?
- How will business interests be treated?
- What about the marital home?

### Step 4: Each Party Hires Independent Counsel

Each person gets their own attorney to:
- Explain their rights under state law (what they'd get WITHOUT a prenup)
- Review the agreement for fairness
- Advise on any problematic provisions
- Ensure their client understands what they're giving up

### Step 5: Draft, Review, Negotiate, Finalize

- Draft the agreement (using our template as a starting point)
- Each attorney reviews with their client
- Negotiate any disputed terms
- Revise until both parties agree
- Wait at least 7 days after final version before signing (California requirement, good practice everywhere)

### Step 6: Execute Properly

- Both parties sign voluntarily
- Notarize
- Add witnesses (if state requires)
- Each party keeps an original
- Attach financial disclosure schedules

## State-Specific Considerations

### Community Property States (9 states + DC)
Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin

In these states, ALL income earned during marriage is automatically community property (50/50). A prenup is the ONLY way to change this default.

### Equitable Distribution States (41 states)
Everything else. Courts divide property "fairly" (not necessarily equally). Factors include: length of marriage, contributions, future needs, etc.

### Notable State Rules:

**California:** 7-day waiting period between receiving final draft and signing. Independent counsel OR written waiver required. Full disclosure mandatory.

**New York:** Generally very enforceable if properly executed. No independent counsel requirement but strongly recommended.

**Florida:** Full financial disclosure required. Must be signed voluntarily. Cannot be unconscionable.

**Texas:** Community property state — prenup is critical to keep separate property separate during marriage.

## Common Prenup Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Presenting It Too Late
Dropping a prenup on your partner a week before the wedding creates a duress argument. Courts have invalidated agreements signed under time pressure. Start 3-6 months early.

### Mistake 2: Hiding Assets in the Disclosure
Undisclosed assets can void the entire agreement. Full transparency is both legally required and relationally healthy.

### Mistake 3: Making It Entirely One-Sided
A prenup that gives one spouse nothing while the other keeps everything is likely unconscionable. Include reasonable provisions for both parties.

### Mistake 4: Including Child Custody Provisions
Courts determine custody based on the child's best interest AT THE TIME of divorce. You cannot pre-determine custody in a prenup — and including such terms may compromise the entire agreement's credibility.

### Mistake 5: Never Updating It
A prenup drafted when both spouses were 25 with minimal assets may be wildly inappropriate 20 years later. Consider periodic reviews, especially after major life changes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Will a prenup protect me if my spouse runs up credit card debt?

If your prenup specifies that each party is solely responsible for debts they individually incur, yes — as between YOU AND YOUR SPOUSE. However, creditors who extended credit to both of you (joint accounts) can still pursue both parties regardless of what the prenup says. A prenup governs between spouses; it cannot override third-party creditor rights.

### Can I write my own prenup without a lawyer?

Legally, yes. Practically, the enforceability risk increases substantially without attorney involvement. At minimum, each party should have an attorney REVIEW the final document before signing, even if you draft it yourselves using a template.

### How much does a prenup typically cost with attorneys?

Ranges: Simple prenups ($1,500-$3,000 total). Moderate complexity ($3,000-$7,000 total). Complex (significant business, multiple properties, blended families): $7,000-$15,000+. These costs protect potentially millions in assets.

### Can a prenup be changed after marriage?

Yes — it's called a "postnuptial agreement" or "amendment." Both parties must agree to changes in writing. Some states treat postnuptial agreements with more scrutiny than prenups. A formal amendment is better than informally ignoring the prenup.

### Does a prenup mean the marriage will fail?

No more than car insurance means you'll have an accident. Financial clarity actually STRENGTHENS marriages by eliminating money-related conflicts and misunderstandings. Studies show couples who discuss finances openly before marriage have lower divorce rates.

### Can a prenup address infidelity?

Some states allow "lifestyle clauses" (penalties for infidelity, drug use, etc.). Enforcement varies dramatically. California generally won't enforce them. Some other states may. Courts view these skeptically. Focus your prenup on financial terms, which are reliably enforceable.
