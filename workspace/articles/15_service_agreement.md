## What Is a Service Agreement?

A Service Agreement (also called a Freelance Contract, Independent Contractor Agreement, or Client Services Agreement) is a legally binding contract between a person or company providing services (the "Provider" or "Contractor") and the person or company receiving those services (the "Client"). It establishes the scope of work, compensation, timeline, ownership of work product, and both parties' rights and obligations.

**Why this differs from an employment contract:** A Service Agreement explicitly establishes the worker as an independent contractor — NOT an employee. This distinction matters enormously for taxes, benefits, liability, and workers' rights. The agreement's terms must reflect true independent contractor status (see Section 4 of our template).

### Section 4: Independent Contractor Status

Critical for legal compliance — this section establishes the worker is NOT an employee:

- Provider controls HOW and WHEN work is performed
- Provider uses their own equipment and workspace
- Provider may work for other clients simultaneously
- Provider is responsible for their own taxes (self-employment tax, estimated payments)
- Client will issue Form 1099-NEC (not W-2)
- No employee benefits provided (health insurance, PTO, retirement)
- Provider maintains their own business insurance

**IRS classification factors addressed:**
- Behavioral control: Provider determines method
- Financial control: Provider sets rates, has own expenses, can profit or lose
- Relationship type: Temporary project, not indefinite employment

### Section 5: Intellectual Property (Choose One)

**Option A: Work Made for Hire (Client Owns Everything)**
All work product is "work made for hire." Client owns all rights immediately upon creation. Provider retains no rights — cannot use in portfolio without written permission.

**Option B: Licensed to Client (Provider Retains Ownership)**
Provider retains copyright and ownership. Client receives a [perpetual / limited] [exclusive / non-exclusive] license to use deliverables for [SPECIFIED PURPOSES]. Provider may display work in their portfolio.

**Option C: Rights Transfer Upon Full Payment**
Provider owns all work until final payment is received. Upon full payment, rights transfer to Client automatically. If Client fails to pay, Provider retains all rights.

**Pre-existing work:** Any tools, frameworks, code libraries, or templates the Provider brings to the project remain the Provider's property. Client receives a license to use them within the deliverables.

## How to Fill Out Your Service Agreement (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Define the Scope Precisely

The #1 source of freelancer-client disputes: unclear scope. Before filling in the agreement:
- List every specific deliverable
- Define what "done" looks like for each
- State what is explicitly NOT included
- Estimate timeline realistically (then add 20% buffer)

### Step 2: Choose Your Compensation Structure

- **Retainer** if: Ongoing relationship, consistent monthly work, client wants availability
- **Project-based** if: Clear deliverables, defined start/end, one-time engagement
- **Hourly** if: Scope is uncertain, discovery/consulting work, client prefers paying for actual time

### Step 3: Address IP Before Starting Work

The biggest mistake freelancers make: not discussing who owns the work until after it's created. Decide NOW:
- Does the client own everything? (They'll pay more for full ownership)
- Do you retain rights? (You charge less but can reuse/license)
- Transfer on payment? (Protects you from non-payment)

### Step 4: Set Revision Limits

Without limits, clients request unlimited changes until the project becomes unprofitable. Standard:
- Design: 2-3 rounds of revisions
- Writing: 1-2 rounds
- Development: Bug fixes included, feature changes are new scope

### Step 5: Establish Payment Terms That Protect You

- Always get money upfront (25-50% deposit for projects)
- Never deliver final files before final payment
- Include a late payment clause with interest
- Reserve the right to pause work when payment is overdue

### Step 6: Both Parties Sign

- Provider signs
- Client signs (authorized representative if a company)
- Date both signatures
- Each party keeps a copy
- Begin work only AFTER the signed agreement is in hand

## Common Service Agreement Mistakes

### Mistake 1: No Written Agreement at All
"We discussed it over email" is not a contract. Verbal agreements are almost impossible to enforce when disputes arise about scope, payment, or deliverables.

### Mistake 2: Vague Scope of Work
"Build a website" means completely different things to different people. Specify pages, features, functionality, content responsibility, and what's explicitly excluded.

### Mistake 3: No Payment Schedule or Deposit
Starting a $10,000 project without upfront payment means you absorb all the risk. If the client disappears after 80% of the work is done, you've worked for free.

### Mistake 4: Unlimited Revisions
"We'll refine it until you're happy" sounds client-friendly but can turn a $2,000 project into 200 hours of unpaid changes. Cap revisions; charge for extras.

### Mistake 5: Not Addressing Scope Creep in Writing
"While you're at it, can you also..." is how scope doubles without payment doubling. Your agreement should require written change orders for ANY work outside the original scope.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do freelancers really need a contract for every client?

Yes — for every project over $500 and every ongoing relationship. Even for smaller projects, a simple agreement prevents misunderstandings. The contracts that seem unnecessary are always the ones you wish you had when things go wrong.

### Should the freelancer or the client provide the contract?

Either can — but the party who provides it usually has terms more favorable to them. As a freelancer, providing YOUR template means your IP, payment, and termination terms are established from the start. As a client, providing yours means scope and deliverable clarity.

### What if a client won't sign a contract?

That's a red flag. Clients who refuse written agreements often: dispute scope later, delay payment, or expect unlimited work. Consider it a screening tool — good clients appreciate professionalism and transparency.

### Can I use one service agreement for all my clients?

You can use the same template, but customize scope, deliverables, rates, and IP terms for each engagement. Our template is designed for repeated use with project-specific details filled in each time.

### What happens if the client doesn't pay?

Steps: (1) Send formal payment reminder, (2) Apply late fees per your agreement, (3) Pause all work, (4) Send demand letter, (5) File in small claims court (for smaller amounts) or hire a collections attorney. Your right to withhold deliverables until payment is your strongest leverage — which is why IP-transfer-upon-payment (Option C) protects freelancers best.

### How do I handle scope creep without damaging the client relationship?

Reference your agreement: "I'd be happy to add that feature. Per our agreement, work outside the original scope requires a change order. I'll send you a quick estimate for the additional work." Professional, not adversarial. Most clients respect this — it shows you value your time AND theirs.
