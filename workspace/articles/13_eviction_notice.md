## What Is an Eviction Notice?

An eviction notice (legally called a "Notice to Quit," "Notice to Vacate," or "Notice to Cure") is a formal written communication from a landlord to a tenant informing them that they must either resolve a lease violation or vacate the property within a specified timeframe.

**Critical distinction:** An eviction notice is NOT an eviction. It's the legally required FIRST STEP in the eviction process. If the tenant doesn't comply with the notice, the landlord must then file a formal eviction lawsuit (called "unlawful detainer," "forcible entry and detainer," or "summary process" depending on the state).

**The eviction process timeline:**
1. **Notice period** (3-60 days depending on type and state) ← YOU ARE HERE
2. Tenant fails to comply
3. Landlord files eviction lawsuit with the court
4. Court hearing (tenant can contest)
5. Judge issues judgment for landlord (or tenant)
6. Writ of possession issued
7. Sheriff/constable physically removes tenant (if still present)

**You CANNOT skip steps.** Self-help eviction (changing locks, removing belongings, shutting off utilities) is ILLEGAL in all 50 states and can result in significant damages awarded to the tenant.

## Types of Eviction Notices (Which Do You Need?)

### 3-Day Notice to Pay Rent or Quit

**Use when:** Tenant hasn't paid rent by the due date (plus any grace period in your lease).

**What it requires:** Tenant must pay ALL overdue rent within 3 days OR vacate the property.

**Key details:**
- Must state the exact amount owed (don't overstate — include ONLY rent, not late fees in most states)
- Must identify the rental period(s) the rent covers
- Must provide information on how/where to pay
- Some states require 5 days (FL, IN) or 7 days (GA, PA) instead of 3

**If tenant pays within the notice period:** You MUST accept payment and cannot proceed with eviction. The notice is cured.

**If tenant doesn't pay or vacate:** You may file the eviction lawsuit on day 4 (or the day after your state's required period expires).

### 14-Day Notice to Cure or Quit

**Use when:** Tenant is violating a lease term (but not through non-payment).

**Common violations:**
- Unauthorized pets
- Unauthorized occupants
- Excessive noise complaints
- Property damage
- Smoking in a non-smoking unit
- Running a business in a residential unit
- Hoarding/health code violations
- Unauthorized alterations

**What it requires:** Tenant must CURE (fix) the violation within 14 days OR vacate.

**Key details:**
- Must describe the specific violation clearly
- Must reference the lease clause being violated
- Must explain what "cure" means (e.g., "remove the unauthorized dog" or "repair the broken window")
- Some violations may be "incurable" (especially repeated violations) — different notice rules apply

### 30-Day Notice to Vacate (No-Fault)

**Use when:** You want to end a month-to-month tenancy without the tenant having done anything wrong.

**What it requires:** Tenant must vacate within 30 days. No violation needs to exist.

**When this applies:**
- Month-to-month tenancies (NOT fixed-term leases — you can't terminate a fixed-term early without cause)
- Landlord wants to renovate, sell, or use the property themselves
- Landlord is not renewing at lease expiration (giving advance notice)

**Important: "Just Cause" jurisdictions override this.** In cities/states with just cause eviction (California's AB 1482, many cities like San Francisco, NYC, Portland, Seattle), you CANNOT give a no-fault notice without a qualifying reason — even month-to-month.

### 60-Day Notice to Vacate

**Required in:**
- California: When tenant has lived there over 1 year (AB 1482)
- Some local jurisdictions with extended notice requirements
- Situations involving elderly or disabled tenants in certain states
- Some government-subsidized housing programs

### Unconditional Quit Notice

**Use when:** The violation is so severe that the tenant gets NO opportunity to cure:
- Illegal drug manufacturing on premises
- Assault or criminal activity against other tenants
- Causing serious damage to the property
- Repeated violations (same violation occurred within previous [6/12] months)
- Health and safety hazards

**Timeframe:** Usually 3-5 days, no cure option.

## How to Properly Serve an Eviction Notice (Critical)

**Improper service = case dismissed.** Courts strictly enforce service requirements. If you can't prove the tenant actually received the notice, your eviction lawsuit fails — even if the tenant clearly saw it.

### Method 1: Personal Service (Strongest)

Hand the notice directly to the tenant (any named tenant on the lease).
- Best: Have a third party do it (they can testify as a witness)
- Document: Date, time, location, who received it, who served it

### Method 2: Substituted Service + Mailing

If the tenant isn't home:
1. Leave the notice with a "person of suitable age and discretion" at the property (adult roommate, family member over 18)
2. ALSO mail a copy to the tenant via regular first-class mail
- Both steps are required — one alone is insufficient

### Method 3: Post and Mail (Last Resort)

If no one answers after multiple attempts:
1. Post (tape/affix) the notice to the front door in a conspicuous location
2. ALSO mail a copy via first-class mail
3. Some states require certified mail as well
- Document your attempts at personal service before resorting to this

## Common Eviction Notice Mistakes (That Get Cases Dismissed)

### Mistake 1: Wrong Notice Period
Using a 3-day notice in a state that requires 5 or 7 days. Or using a 30-day notice when 60 is required. Always verify YOUR state's specific requirement.

### Mistake 2: Including Late Fees in the Amount Owed
Most states require the 3-day notice to state ONLY the unpaid rent — not late fees, utilities, damages, or other charges. Overstating the amount gives the tenant grounds to contest.

### Mistake 3: Improper or Undocumented Service
Sliding the notice under the door without documentation. Texting a photo of the notice. Telling the tenant verbally. None of these constitute proper legal service in most states.

### Mistake 4: Accepting Partial Payment After Notice
In many states, accepting ANY payment after serving a pay-or-quit notice "waives" the notice — you must start over. Know your state's rules on partial payment.

### Mistake 5: Serving a No-Fault Notice in a Just-Cause Jurisdiction
If your property falls under just-cause eviction protections (California AB 1482, Seattle, Portland, NYC rent stabilization), a 30-day no-fault notice is VOID unless you have a qualifying reason (owner move-in, major renovation, etc.).

### Mistake 6: Retaliatory Eviction
Evicting a tenant within [30-180] days of them filing a complaint (health/safety, code violation, organized tenant activity) creates a presumption of retaliation — which is illegal. Even if you have legitimate grounds, the timing may doom your case.

## After the Notice Period: Next Steps

### If Tenant Complies:
- **Pays rent:** Accept payment, keep a record, and the matter is resolved
- **Cures violation:** Document the cure, file notice for your records, monitor for repeat
- **Vacates:** Conduct move-out inspection, process security deposit per state law

### If Tenant Does NOT Comply:
1. **Day after notice expires:** You may file the eviction lawsuit (unlawful detainer)
2. **Court filing:** Submit complaint, pay filing fees ($50-$400 depending on jurisdiction)
3. **Service of summons:** Tenant is formally notified of the lawsuit
4. **Hearing:** Typically 5-20 days after filing
5. **Judgment:** If you win, court issues judgment for possession (and potentially back rent)
6. **Writ of possession:** Sheriff gives tenant final notice (usually 5-10 days)
7. **Lock-out:** Sheriff physically removes tenant if they haven't left

**Total timeline (filing to removal):** 3-8 weeks in tenant-friendly states; 2-4 weeks in landlord-friendly states.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much notice do I have to give for non-payment of rent?

It depends on your state — ranging from immediate (Georgia) to 14 days (New York, Washington). Most states require 3-5 days. Always check your specific state's requirements AND your lease terms (which may provide a longer period than the statutory minimum).

### Can I evict a tenant without a written notice?

No — proper written notice is a legal prerequisite to filing an eviction lawsuit in every state. Even if the tenant knows they owe rent, a court will dismiss your case without proof of proper written notice and service.

### What if the tenant pays partial rent during the notice period?

This varies significantly by state. In some states (California), accepting partial payment waives the notice entirely. In others, you may accept partial payment and still proceed. Know your state's rules BEFORE the situation arises.

### Can I change the locks or shut off utilities to force a tenant out?

Absolutely not. "Self-help eviction" is illegal in all 50 states. Penalties include: statutory damages ($1,000-$10,000+), actual damages, attorney fees, and potentially criminal charges. Always go through the court process.

### How long does the entire eviction process take?

From notice to physical removal: typically 5-12 weeks total. Breakdown: notice period (3-60 days) + filing to hearing (1-3 weeks) + hearing to judgment (same day to 1 week) + judgment to removal (5-10 days). Tenant-friendly states (California, New York) tend toward the longer end.

### Can a tenant stop an eviction by paying after the notice expires?

In most states: no, once the notice period has expired without payment and you've filed the lawsuit, you're not required to accept late payment. However, some states give tenants a "right of redemption" allowing them to pay and stay even after the lawsuit is filed (typically at the first court hearing). Check your state's rules.
