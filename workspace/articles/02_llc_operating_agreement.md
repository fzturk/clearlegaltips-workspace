## What Is an LLC Operating Agreement?

An LLC Operating Agreement is the internal governance document that defines how your Limited Liability Company operates. Think of it as your LLC's rulebook — it establishes ownership percentages, profit distribution, management structure, voting rights, and what happens when members join, leave, or pass away.

Unlike your Articles of Organization (which are filed with the state and create the LLC), an Operating Agreement is an internal document. It's typically not filed anywhere — it stays in your records. But its legal importance cannot be overstated.

**Here's what most people don't realize:** Your LLC's liability protection — the very reason you formed an LLC instead of operating as a sole proprietorship — depends partly on having a proper Operating Agreement. Courts have "pierced the corporate veil" (removed LLC liability protection) when businesses failed to maintain proper governance documents, because it suggests the LLC is merely an alter ego of the owner rather than a separate legal entity.

## Why Every LLC Needs an Operating Agreement (Even Single-Member LLCs)

### Reason 1: Liability Protection (Veil Piercing Defense)

Without an Operating Agreement, courts may conclude your LLC isn't really a separate entity from you personally. This is called "piercing the veil," and it means creditors can go after your personal assets (house, car, savings) for business debts.

An Operating Agreement is evidence that you treat your LLC as a legitimate, separate business entity with formal governance.

### Reason 2: State Default Rules May Not Match Your Intent

Every state has default LLC statutes that apply when you don't have an Operating Agreement. These defaults may not align with what you actually want:

| Issue | Common State Default | What You Might Prefer |
|-------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Profit split | Equal, regardless of investment | Proportional to capital contribution |
| Voting rights | Per-capita (each member = 1 vote) | Proportional to ownership % |
| New members | May require only majority vote | Unanimous consent |
| Transfer of interest | May be freely transferable | Requires member approval |
| Dissolution | May occur on any member's withdrawal | Continues with remaining members |
| Management | All members manage | Designated manager or managers |

**Real-world example:** Two partners form an LLC. Partner A invests $90,000. Partner B invests $10,000. Without an Operating Agreement, their state's default says profits split equally (50/50). Partner A expected to receive 90% of profits matching their 90% investment. This dispute — which an Operating Agreement prevents entirely — now requires expensive litigation.

### Reason 3: Bank Account Requirements

Many banks require a copy of your Operating Agreement to open a business bank account. Without one, you may be forced to use a personal account, which further undermines your liability protection.

### Reason 4: Tax Election Documentation

If your LLC elects S-Corp tax treatment (a common strategy to reduce self-employment tax), the IRS expects to see an Operating Agreement documenting membership, profit allocation, and management — consistent with your tax filings.

### Reason 5: Dispute Resolution Framework

When disagreements arise — and they will — the Operating Agreement provides the resolution framework. Without one, disputes go directly to expensive litigation under state default rules.

The ownership table — who owns what percentage and what they contributed:

| Member | Contribution | Value | Ownership % | Voting % |
|--------|-------------|-------|-------------|----------|
| [Name] | [Cash/Property/Services] | $[Amount] | [__]% | [__]% |

Our template covers all contribution types:
- **Cash contributions** — straightforward dollar amounts
- **Property contributions** — real estate, equipment, vehicles, intellectual property (valued at fair market value)
- **Services contributions** — sweat equity (note: this may trigger immediate tax liability)

**Additional contribution provisions:**
- Whether additional capital calls are permitted
- What happens if a member fails to meet a capital call
- Whether loans from members are allowed and on what terms

### Article III: Allocations & Distributions

**Profit and loss allocation:**
- Default: proportional to ownership percentage
- Alternative: special allocations (must comply with IRC Section 704(b) "substantial economic effect" rules)

**Distribution timing and rules:**
- When distributions occur (monthly, quarterly, annually, or by vote)
- Whether distributions are mandatory or at management's discretion
- Tax distributions (minimum distributions to cover members' tax liabilities on allocated profits)
- Distribution waterfall for complex arrangements

**Critical distinction:** Allocation (how profits/losses appear on tax returns) vs. Distribution (actual cash payments to members). Our template handles both separately because they don't have to match.

### Article IV: Management Structure

**Option A: Member-Managed**

All members participate in management decisions. Each member can bind the LLC in ordinary business matters. Major decisions require a vote (majority or supermajority).

Best for: Small LLCs where all members are actively involved in daily operations.

**Option B: Manager-Managed**

One or more designated managers (who may or may not be members) handle daily operations. Non-manager members are passive investors with limited authority.

Best for: LLCs with passive investors, multiple members where not all are involved in operations, or LLCs that want to bring in professional management.

**Our template includes both options** with pre-written language for each. Simply select your structure and delete the other.

**Decision-making hierarchy:**
- Day-to-day operations: Manager(s) decide without a vote
- Ordinary business decisions: Simple majority of members
- Major decisions requiring supermajority (67% or 75%): Specified in our template

### Article V: Voting Rights

- Voting basis: per-capita vs. proportional to ownership
- Quorum requirements
- Proxy voting rules
- Actions requiring unanimous consent (our template lists 8 common items)
- Written consent in lieu of meeting

### Article VI: Transfer Restrictions

This section prevents unwanted strangers from becoming your business partners:

- **Right of First Refusal (ROFR):** Before selling to an outsider, the departing member must offer their interest to existing members first at the same price.
- **Tag-Along Rights:** If one member sells, other members can require the buyer to also purchase their interests at the same terms.
- **Drag-Along Rights:** If members holding [__]% agree to sell, they can force remaining members to also sell (important for clean exits).
- **Permitted Transfers:** Transfers to family trusts, estate planning entities, or other members are exempt from restrictions.
- **Prohibited Transfers:** Any transfer that would terminate the LLC's tax status, violate securities laws, or result in a disqualifying transfer.

### Article VII: New Members

- Admission requires [unanimous / majority / supermajority] consent
- New member contribution requirements
- Impact on existing ownership percentages (dilution)
- New members must sign and agree to the Operating Agreement

### Article VIII: Withdrawal, Resignation & Expulsion

**Voluntary Withdrawal:**
- Notice period (typically 60-180 days)
- Whether the LLC must buy back the withdrawing member's interest
- Valuation method for buyout (book value, appraised value, formula)
- Payment terms (lump sum vs. installments over 12-60 months)

**Expulsion (Involuntary Removal):**
- Grounds for expulsion (material breach, bankruptcy, criminal conviction, competing business)
- Vote required (typically supermajority excluding the member in question)
- Buyout terms for expelled members (often at a discount to fair value)

### Article IX: Death, Disability & Incapacity

What happens when a member can no longer participate:

- **Buy-Sell Provision:** LLC or remaining members purchase the deceased/disabled member's interest
- **Valuation:** Fixed price, formula, or independent appraisal
- **Funding:** Life insurance and disability insurance recommendations
- **Heirs' Rights:** Whether heirs become members or only receive economic distributions

**This is the section most people skip — and it causes the most expensive disputes.** Without these provisions, a deceased member's interest passes to their estate, potentially giving their heirs (whom you may never have met) a say in your business decisions.

### Article X: Dissolution & Winding Up

Events triggering dissolution:
- Unanimous (or supermajority) vote to dissolve
- Expiration of the LLC's stated term
- Entry of a judicial decree of dissolution
- Events specified in the Articles of Organization

**Winding up process:**
1. Notice to creditors
2. Liquidation of assets
3. Payment of debts and obligations
4. Distribution of remaining assets to members per ownership percentages

Our template includes an **anti-dissolution provision** — the LLC continues even if a member withdraws, dies, or is expelled, unless the required vote to dissolve is obtained. Without this, many state defaults would dissolve the LLC upon any member's departure.

### Article XI: Tax Provisions

- Tax classification election (partnership, S-Corp, or disregarded entity for single-member)
- Tax Matters Partner/Partnership Representative designation
- Requirement to provide K-1 information by specific deadlines
- Tax distribution provisions (ensuring members receive enough cash to pay taxes on allocated income)
- Section 754 election (step-up in basis on transfer)

### Article XII: Indemnification

The LLC indemnifies members and managers against personal liability for actions taken in good faith on behalf of the LLC. Standard carve-outs for fraud, gross negligence, and willful misconduct.

### Article XIII: Books, Records & Reporting

- What records the LLC must maintain
- Members' inspection rights
- Annual financial reporting requirements
- Meeting minutes (if applicable)

## How to Fill Out Your LLC Operating Agreement (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Gather Your Formation Documents

Before filling in the template, have these ready:
- Articles of Organization (as filed with your state)
- EIN confirmation letter from the IRS
- Business bank account details
- List of all members with their legal names and addresses
- Record of each member's contribution (amount, type, date)

### Step 2: Select Your Template Version

**Single-Member LLC:** Use our simplified version — it covers liability protection, management authority, and tax provisions without the multi-member complexity of voting and transfers.

**Multi-Member LLC:** Use the full version with all articles. Don't skip sections thinking "we'll never disagree" — the purpose is precisely to handle the situations you don't expect.

### Step 3: Fill in Formation Details (Article I)

Copy these exactly from your Articles of Organization:
- Official LLC name (including "LLC" or "L.L.C.")
- State and date of formation
- Registered agent information
- Principal office address

### Step 4: Complete the Membership Table (Article II)

For each member, document:
- Full legal name
- Contribution type and amount
- Ownership percentage
- Initial capital account balance

**Important:** Ownership percentages must total exactly 100%. Contributions should be at fair market value for non-cash items.

### Step 5: Choose Management Structure (Article IV)

**Choose Member-Managed if:**
- All members will actively participate in operations
- You have 2-4 members who all want a say
- You don't want the cost of designating a formal manager

**Choose Manager-Managed if:**
- Some members are passive investors
- You want clear authority for daily decisions
- You plan to bring in non-member professional management
- You have 5+ members (democratic management becomes unwieldy)

### Step 6: Set Your Profit & Distribution Terms (Article III)

Decide:
- Whether profits split by ownership % or some other formula
- How often distributions occur
- Whether there's a minimum tax distribution
- Whether the manager(s) or a member vote controls distribution timing

### Step 7: Configure Transfer Restrictions (Article VI)

At minimum, include Right of First Refusal. For multi-member LLCs, also consider:
- Whether to include drag-along/tag-along
- What "permitted transfers" look like (estate planning)
- Whether a member can pledge their interest as collateral

### Step 8: Address Death & Disability (Article IX)

This conversation is uncomfortable but essential. Decide:
- Whether the LLC or remaining members buy the interest
- Valuation method (agree NOW while everyone is rational)
- Payment terms (lump sum is cleanest; installments are more realistic)
- Whether to fund with life insurance (strongly recommended)

### Step 9: Review, Sign, and Store

- All members sign and date
- Each member receives a copy
- Store the original securely (fireproof safe or safety deposit box)
- Give a copy to your accountant and attorney
- **Do NOT file with the state** (it's an internal document)
- Update whenever membership or terms change

## State-by-State Operating Agreement Requirements (2026)

### States That REQUIRE an Operating Agreement by Law:
- **California** — Required (Cal. Corp. Code 17701.10)
- **New York** — Required within 90 days of formation (NY LLC Law 417)
- **Delaware** — Required (6 Del. C. 18-101)
- **Missouri** — Required (Mo. Rev. Stat. 347.081)
- **Maine** — Required (31 MRSA 1541)

### States That Strongly Recommend (But Don't Legally Require):
Every other state — while not technically mandatory, banks, investors, and courts all expect one. Operating without one in ANY state is inadvisable.

### State-Specific Considerations:

**California:** Operating Agreement cannot waive fiduciary duties entirely. Must include specific language regarding member inspection rights.

**New York:** Must be in writing (oral agreements are not valid for NY LLCs). Publication requirement in some counties is separate from the Operating Agreement.

**Texas:** Called a "Company Agreement." Can be oral or written, but written is obviously preferred and virtually required for practical purposes.

**Florida:** Operating Agreement can include provisions limiting fiduciary duties (unlike some states). Allows for "series LLCs" which require complex Operating Agreement provisions.

**Delaware:** Maximum flexibility. Operating Agreement can modify or eliminate fiduciary duties, restrict member rights, and customize governance almost without limit.

## Common Operating Agreement Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Using a Generic Template Without State-Specific Language

Every state has its own LLC Act with unique default rules, permissible modifications, and required provisions. A template designed for Delaware won't address California's publication requirements or New York's mandatory written agreement rule.

### Mistake 2: Not Addressing Member Departure

Without exit provisions, a departing member may have the legal right to force dissolution of the entire LLC. Our template includes continuation provisions and buyout procedures specifically to prevent this.

### Mistake 3: Oral Agreements Instead of Written Updates

When members informally agree to change profit splits or responsibilities, those changes may not be enforceable — or worse, may contradict the written Operating Agreement. Our template includes an amendment procedure. Use it every time you make a change.

### Mistake 4: Failing to Match Tax Elections

If your Operating Agreement says profits split 60/40 but you're filing taxes showing 50/50 (or vice versa), you're inviting an IRS audit. Your Operating Agreement, tax filings, and actual distributions should be consistent.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring the Operating Agreement After Signing

An Operating Agreement isn't a "file and forget" document. Review it annually. Update it when circumstances change. Follow its procedures (hold required meetings, maintain proper records, make distributions as scheduled). Failing to follow your own Operating Agreement can be used against you in veil-piercing claims.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does a single-member LLC need an Operating Agreement?

Absolutely. While there's no second member to agree with, a single-member Operating Agreement: (1) documents that the LLC is a separate entity from you (critical for veil-piercing defense), (2) is often required by banks to open a business account, (3) establishes how the LLC is managed and what happens if you become incapacitated, and (4) is required by law in states like California, New York, and Delaware.

### Can I write my own LLC Operating Agreement?

Yes. There's no legal requirement that an attorney draft it. However, the document must properly address your state's LLC laws and your specific situation. Using a comprehensive template (like ours) is far safer than writing from scratch, because it ensures you don't miss critical provisions.

### What happens if I don't have an Operating Agreement?

Your state's default LLC statute governs your LLC completely. This means equal profit splits regardless of investment amounts, potential dissolution when any member leaves, and uncertainty about management authority. You also face a higher risk of veil-piercing in lawsuits.

### Can an Operating Agreement be amended?

Yes. Our template includes an amendment provision specifying the required vote (typically unanimous for single-member or supermajority for multi-member). All amendments should be in writing, signed by the required members, and attached to the original document.

### Is an Operating Agreement the same as Articles of Organization?

No. Articles of Organization (or Certificate of Formation) is the PUBLIC document filed with the state to create the LLC. The Operating Agreement is a PRIVATE internal document governing how the LLC operates. Both are necessary, but they serve different purposes.

### Do I file the Operating Agreement with the state?

In most states, no — it stays in your records. A few states (like California) require you to maintain it at the principal office for member inspection, but even California doesn't require filing. Exception: Some states allow voluntary filing, and banks or investors may request a certified copy.

### Can an Operating Agreement override state law?

In many areas, yes. Most state LLC Acts are "default rules" that apply only when the Operating Agreement is silent. Your Operating Agreement can customize voting thresholds, restrict transfers, modify fiduciary duties (in states like Delaware), change dissolution events, and more. However, certain protections (like member access to records or judicial dissolution rights) typically cannot be waived.
