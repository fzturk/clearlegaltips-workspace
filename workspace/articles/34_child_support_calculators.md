## Three Child Support Calculation Models

### Income Shares Model (41 States)
Based on the combined income of BOTH parents. The idea: the child should receive the same proportion of parental income they would have received if the family stayed together.

**Formula:** Combined parental income → lookup table → total support obligation → split proportional to each parent's income share.

### Percentage of Income Model (6 States)
Based on the NON-CUSTODIAL parent's income only. A fixed percentage applies based on the number of children.

**States using this model:** Alaska, Mississippi, Nevada, North Dakota, Texas, Wisconsin

**Texas example:**
| Number of Children | % of Net Income |
|:-----------------:|:--------------:|
| 1 child | 20% |
| 2 children | 25% |
| 3 children | 30% |
| 4 children | 35% |
| 5+ children | 40% (max) |

### Melson Formula (3 States)
A modified income shares model with additional steps: ensures each parent retains enough for basic self-support needs before calculating child support.

**States using this model:** Delaware, Hawaii, Montana

## Factors That Affect Child Support Calculations

### Standard Factors (All States)

| Factor | How It Affects Support |
|--------|:--------------------:|
| Both parents' gross income | Primary calculation input |
| Number of children | More children = higher obligation |
| Custody arrangement (overnights) | More shared time = lower obligation |
| Health insurance costs | Added to basic obligation |
| Childcare costs | Added to basic obligation |
| Pre-existing child support orders | Deducted from available income |

| Factor | States That Consider It |

## How to Estimate Your Child Support

### Step 1: Determine Both Parents' Gross Income
Include: salary, wages, commissions, bonuses, overtime, self-employment income, rental income, investment income, Social Security, disability, unemployment, pension/retirement income.

Exclude (usually): means-tested benefits (TANF, SSI, food stamps), income of new spouse, gifts.

### Step 2: Apply Allowed Deductions
Common deductions that reduce "available income":
- Federal and state income taxes
- FICA/self-employment tax
- Mandatory retirement contributions
- Union dues
- Health insurance premiums (for the parent)
- Prior child support obligations
- Spousal support paid

### Step 3: Use Your State's Calculator
Input both parents' adjusted income, number of children, custody schedule (overnights per year), health insurance cost, and childcare cost into your state's official calculator.

### Step 4: Understand the Result
The calculator provides a GUIDELINE amount. Courts can deviate based on special circumstances (child with special needs, high-income parents, extraordinary expenses).

## Modifying Child Support

Child support can be modified when there's a "substantial change in circumstances":

- **Income change:** Job loss, promotion, pay cut, disability
- **Custody change:** Change in overnights or primary custody
- **Healthcare cost change:** Insurance changes, new medical needs
- **Additional children:** New children in either household
- **Child's needs change:** Special education, medical conditions

**Most states allow modification review every 2-3 years** even without a specific "change" — to ensure the order reflects current incomes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much child support will I pay for one child?

It depends on your state, both parents' incomes, and the custody arrangement. General estimates for one child: 15-25% of the non-custodial parent's net income (percentage states) or a guideline amount based on combined income (income shares states). Use your state's official calculator for an accurate estimate.

### Does joint custody mean no child support?

Not necessarily. Even in 50/50 custody arrangements, if there's a significant income disparity, the higher-earning parent typically pays some child support. The amount is reduced based on equal overnights, but rarely eliminated entirely.

### Can child support be waived?

Parents cannot waive child support — it's the child's right, not the parent's. Courts can set child support at $0 in rare circumstances (truly equal income and equal custody), but neither parent can contractually waive the other's obligation.

### What happens if I don't pay child support?

Enforcement actions include: wage garnishment, tax refund intercept, license suspension (driver's, professional, recreational), passport denial, bank account levy, liens on property, contempt of court (jail time), and credit reporting. Child support enforcement is aggressive and effective.

### Does my new spouse's income affect child support?

In most states: no. New spouse's income is not included in the child support calculation. However, a new spouse may affect the parent's "ability to pay" argument if the new spouse covers household expenses, freeing more income for support.
