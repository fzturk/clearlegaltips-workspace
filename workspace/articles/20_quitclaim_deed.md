## What Is a Quitclaim Deed?

A quitclaim deed (sometimes incorrectly called a "quit claim deed") is a legal document that transfers whatever ownership interest the grantor (person giving up the property) has in a piece of real estate to the grantee (person receiving the property) — without any warranties or guarantees about the quality of that interest.

**The critical distinction:** A quitclaim deed transfers only what the grantor ACTUALLY owns — which could be full ownership, partial ownership, or nothing at all. If the grantor has no legal interest in the property, the grantee receives nothing, and has no legal recourse against the grantor.

**Quitclaim vs. Warranty Deed:**

| Feature | Quitclaim Deed | General Warranty Deed |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| Title guarantee | None | Full — grantor warrants clear title |
| Protection for buyer | None | Grantor liable if title defects found |
| Title search required? | Not legally, but recommended | Always performed |
| Title insurance | Rarely issued | Standard requirement |
| Speed | Fast — minimal requirements | Slower — title search, insurance |
| Cost | $0-$200 (filing fees only) | $500-$2,000+ (search, insurance) |
| Common use | Family transfers, divorce, trusts | Real estate sales to third parties |
| Risk level | High for buyer | Low for buyer |

**Special Warranty Deed** falls between these: The grantor warrants title only for the period they owned the property — not before. Common in commercial real estate and bank-owned properties.

## When Do You Need a Quitclaim Deed?

### Family Transfers

1. **Between spouses during marriage** — Adding a spouse to the title of a home purchased before marriage, or transferring property into joint ownership.

2. **Divorce property division** — One spouse quitclaims their interest to the other as part of the divorce settlement. (This transfers ownership but does NOT remove you from the mortgage — see FAQ.)

3. **Gift to family members** — Parent transferring property to a child, or between siblings. Gift tax may apply for transfers above the annual exclusion.

4. **Inheritance outside probate** — Transferring property from an estate to an heir when a simple transfer is appropriate.

### Trust and Estate Planning

5. **Transfer to a living trust** — Moving property from your personal name into your revocable living trust to avoid probate. (You quitclaim from "John Smith" to "John Smith, Trustee of the John Smith Living Trust.")

6. **Transfer from trust to individual** — When dissolving a trust or distributing trust assets to beneficiaries.

### Business and Legal

7. **Transfer to your LLC or corporation** — Moving personal real estate into a business entity for liability protection or investment management.

8. **Correcting a title defect** — Fixing a misspelled name, adding a middle initial, or correcting a legal description on a prior deed.

9. **Removing a deceased person** — When a joint tenant dies, the surviving owner may use a quitclaim (or affidavit of survivorship, depending on the state) to clear the deceased's name from title.

10. **Cloud removal** — Someone who may have a claim to the property (through an old deed, lien, or inheritance) quitclaims their interest to clear the title.

## How to Complete and File a Quitclaim Deed (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Get the Current Legal Description

This is the most critical step. The legal description on your quitclaim deed must EXACTLY match the property's legal description. Sources:
- Your current deed (from when you purchased or received the property)
- County recorder's website (search by address or parcel number)
- Title report or title insurance policy
- Property tax statement (usually has the parcel number)

DO NOT use the street address as the legal description. "123 Main Street" is an informal reference, not a legal description.

### Step 2: Identify All Parties Correctly

- Use the grantor's name EXACTLY as it appears on the current deed
- If the name has changed (marriage, legal name change), include both: "Jane Smith, formerly known as Jane Doe"
- If multiple grantors: ALL must sign

### Step 3: Determine How Grantees Will Hold Title

If transferring to multiple grantees, they must specify how they'll co-own:
- **Joint tenancy with right of survivorship:** When one owner dies, their share automatically passes to the surviving owner(s) — bypasses probate
- **Tenants in common:** Each owns a specified percentage; their share goes to their estate, not the co-owner
- **Community property:** Available in 9 states for married couples

### Step 4: Complete the Form

Fill in all blanks, including:
- Prepared by / return to
- Grantor and grantee names and addresses
- Consideration amount
- Full legal description
- County and state
- Tax parcel number

### Step 5: Sign Before a Notary

The grantor must sign in the physical presence of a notary public. The notary:
- Verifies the grantor's identity (government-issued ID required)
- Confirms the grantor is signing voluntarily
- Affixes notarial seal and signature
- If your state requires witnesses, they must be present at signing too

### Step 6: Record the Deed

File the signed, notarized deed with the county recorder's office:
- In person at the recorder's office
- By mail with the recording fee (check payable to the county)
- Online in some counties (electronic recording)

**Recording fee:** Typically $10-$50 for the first page, $3-$10 per additional page.

**An unrecorded deed is still valid** between the parties — but recording protects the grantee from future claims and establishes public notice of the transfer.

### Step 7: Handle Transfer Taxes and Tax Reporting

- **Transfer tax (documentary stamp tax):** Many states charge a tax based on the property's value or sale price. Exemptions often apply for family transfers, divorces, and trust transfers.
- **Gift tax reporting:** Transfers without fair market value consideration may require IRS Form 709 (Gift Tax Return) if the value exceeds the annual exclusion ($19,000 for 2026).
- **Property tax reassessment:** Some states reassess property value upon transfer. Many exempt transfers between spouses, parents/children (California Prop 19 with restrictions).

## Common Quitclaim Deed Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Wrong Legal Description
A deed with an incorrect legal description may be rejected for recording — or worse, may transfer the wrong property. Always copy the legal description from the existing deed or a title report. Never paraphrase.

### Mistake 2: Assuming the Mortgage Transfers Too
Quitclaiming your interest in a property does NOT remove you from the mortgage. If your name is on the loan, you're still liable for the debt. The mortgage is a separate contract with the lender. To be removed from the mortgage, the remaining owner must refinance in their name alone.

### Mistake 3: Not Recording the Deed
An unrecorded deed leaves the grantee vulnerable. Without recording, a subsequent buyer or creditor could claim the property — and a "race-notice" recording state would protect the new buyer over the original grantee.

### Mistake 4: Using a Quitclaim When You Need a Warranty Deed
If you're buying property from a stranger, NEVER accept a quitclaim deed. You need a warranty deed with title insurance. A quitclaim provides zero protection if the seller doesn't actually own clear title.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring Tax Consequences
Transfers can trigger gift tax obligations, property tax reassessment, and loss of stepped-up basis for capital gains. Consult a tax professional before transferring property, especially for high-value real estate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does a quitclaim deed remove me from the mortgage?

No — this is the single most common misconception. A deed transfers ownership (title). A mortgage is a separate loan agreement with the lender. Quitclaiming your property interest means you still owe the mortgage. To remove yourself from the mortgage, the other party must refinance. If they can't refinance, the lender may invoke the due-on-sale clause and demand full repayment.

### Do I need title insurance with a quitclaim deed?

Title insurance companies rarely insure quitclaim deed transfers because there's no title warranty. For family transfers, this is usually acceptable. For any transfer from a non-family member, insist on a warranty deed AND title insurance.

### How long does a quitclaim deed take to process?

Recording typically takes 1-4 weeks depending on the county. The transfer is effective upon delivery to the grantee (or upon signing in many jurisdictions) — recording establishes the public record but doesn't create the transfer.

### Can I quitclaim property to avoid creditors?

Transferring property to avoid creditors is called a "fraudulent conveyance" and can be reversed by the court. Creditors can challenge transfers made without fair consideration within the lookback period (typically 2-4 years, up to 10 years for federal tax liens). This strategy doesn't work and can result in additional penalties.

### Can I reverse a quitclaim deed?

Only if the grantee voluntarily quitclaims the property back to you, OR you successfully challenge the deed in court (fraud, duress, incapacity, forgery). Once recorded, a quitclaim deed is presumed valid. Think carefully before signing.

### Do both spouses need to sign a quitclaim deed?

Only the grantor (the person giving up interest) needs to sign. However, in many states, if the property is the marital homestead, the non-grantor spouse must also sign or consent — even if they're not on the title. Check your state's homestead and marital property laws.
