## When to Use Each Filing Method

### DIY Filing Works For:
- Simple word marks (business name only — no logo)
- Goods/services that fit neatly into pre-approved descriptions
- Budget-constrained startups willing to accept higher rejection risk
- Applicants comfortable navigating government forms
- Marks with no obvious conflicts (you've searched and found nothing similar)

### Online Service Works For:
- Most small businesses and entrepreneurs
- Standard trademark applications (word marks and simple logos)
- Businesses wanting professional search without full attorney cost
- Applications where the goods/services description is straightforward

### Attorney Recommended For:
- Logos or design marks (more complex applications)
- Names that are similar to existing marks (navigating Office Actions)
- International filings (Madrid Protocol)
- Brands with significant investment (high stakes = high value of protection)
- Multi-class applications
- Businesses in crowded trademark classes (tech, clothing, food)
- Any situation involving a likelihood of confusion refusal

## Trademark Search Before Filing

A trademark search identifies existing registrations that could conflict with yours. Filing without searching wastes your filing fee if a conflict exists.

### Free Search Tools
- **USPTO TESS database** — Searches registered and pending marks
- **Google search** — Finds unregistered common-law marks
- **State trademark databases** — State-level registrations
- **Domain name search** — Identifies businesses using similar names

### Professional Search ($100-$1,000)
- Comprehensive database search across federal, state, and common-law marks
- Analysis of potential conflicts
- Opinion on likelihood of confusion
- Recommended by trademark attorneys before every filing

## Common Trademark Filing Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Filing Without Searching
The #1 cause of rejection: filing a mark that's too similar to an existing registration. A $250-$500 professional search can save you the $250 filing fee plus months of wasted time.

### Mistake 2: Choosing the Wrong Filing Basis
"Use in commerce" (Section 1a) = you're already using the mark in business
"Intent to use" (Section 1b) = you plan to use it but haven't started yet
Filing the wrong basis creates complications and may require additional fees.

### Mistake 3: Too Broad or Too Narrow Goods Description
Too broad: examiner refuses and requires narrowing (Office Action). Too narrow: you don't protect all your planned goods/services. Getting the description right the first time avoids complications.

### Mistake 4: Filing for the Wrong Type of Mark
Word mark (text only) gives broadest protection — covers the name in any font/style. Design mark (logo) only protects that specific visual design. When in doubt, file the word mark first.

### Mistake 5: Ignoring the Office Action
50% of applications receive an Office Action. You have 3 months to respond. Ignoring it = your application is abandoned and your filing fee is lost. No refunds.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a trademark worth the cost for a small business?

If your business name/brand has value and you plan to grow, yes. A trademark prevents competitors from using your name, gives you legal recourse if someone copies your brand, and builds equity in your business identity. The $250-$1,000 cost protects a potentially million-dollar brand asset.

### How long does trademark protection last?

Indefinitely — as long as you continue using the mark and file required maintenance documents (Section 8 Declaration at years 5-6, Section 9 Renewal every 10 years). Unlike patents (20 years) or copyrights (life + 70 years), trademarks can last forever.

### Can I trademark a name I'm not using yet?

Yes — file an "Intent to Use" (ITU) application. You have up to 3 years after approval to begin using the mark in commerce (with extensions at $125/class every 6 months). You must actually use it before registration issues.

### What's the difference between TM and ®?

TM (™) can be used by anyone claiming trademark rights — no registration required. ® (registered) can ONLY be used after your mark is officially registered with the USPTO. Using ® before registration is illegal and can jeopardize your application.

### Do I need a trademark if I already have an LLC?

LLC registration protects your business name within your state only. A federal trademark protects your brand name nationwide across all 50 states. Different businesses with the same LLC name can exist in different states — but only one can own the federal trademark.
