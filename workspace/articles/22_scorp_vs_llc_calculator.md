## S-Corp vs LLC Tax Calculator — Find Your Break-Even Point

The #1 reason business owners consider S-Corp election: self-employment tax savings. A standard LLC owner pays 15.3% self-employment tax on ALL net business income. An S-Corp owner pays self-employment tax only on their "reasonable salary" — the remaining profit passes through as distributions, avoiding SE tax entirely.

But S-Corp election isn't free — payroll costs, additional tax filings, and accounting fees eat into savings. Use our calculator framework below to determine whether S-Corp makes sense for YOUR income level.

## How the S-Corp Tax Advantage Works

### LLC (Default Tax Treatment)

As a single-member LLC or multi-member LLC taxed as a partnership, you report ALL net business income on your personal tax return. You pay:

1. **Federal income tax** on net business income (10%-37% depending on bracket)
2. **Self-employment tax** (15.3%) on net business income up to the Social Security wage base ($176,100 for 2026), then 2.9% Medicare on all income above that
3. **State income tax** (0%-13.3% depending on state)

**Example — LLC with $120,000 net income:**
- Self-employment tax: $120,000 x 15.3% = **$18,360**
- (You deduct 50% of SE tax on your income tax return)

### S-Corp (After Election)

As an S-Corp, you're both an owner AND an employee. You must pay yourself a "reasonable salary." Only the salary portion is subject to employment taxes (Social Security + Medicare). The remaining profit is a distribution — not subject to SE/employment tax.

**Same example — S-Corp with $120,000 net income, $60,000 reasonable salary:**
- Employment taxes on salary: $60,000 x 15.3% = **$9,180** (split between employer/employee)
- Distribution (no SE tax): $60,000 x 0% = **$0**
- **SE tax saved: $9,180**

## When S-Corp Election Makes Sense

### Good Candidates for S-Corp

1. **Established businesses** with consistent net income above $80,000/year
2. **Service professionals** (consultants, doctors, lawyers, accountants) with high personal-service income
3. **Businesses with predictable income** where reasonable salary can be set reliably
4. **Single-owner businesses** where the owner is the primary worker
5. **Businesses already using an accountant** (minimal incremental cost for S-Corp return)

### Bad Candidates for S-Corp

1. **New businesses** with unpredictable or low income (under $60,000)
2. **Businesses with significant losses** (S-Corp loss deduction rules are more restrictive)
3. **Real estate investors** (passive income — SE tax doesn't apply anyway)
4. **Businesses planning to raise equity** (S-Corp restrictions on shareholders and share classes)
5. **Businesses with non-US owners** (S-Corp requires US citizen/resident shareholders)
6. **Businesses with more than 100 owners** (S-Corp shareholder limit)

## How to Make the S-Corp Election

### Step 1: Have an Existing LLC (or Form One)

You don't create a separate S-Corp entity. You form an LLC, then elect S-Corp TAX TREATMENT with the IRS. Your legal structure remains an LLC; only the tax treatment changes.

### Step 2: File IRS Form 2553

- File within 75 days of formation OR by March 15 for the current tax year
- All members/shareholders must consent
- Choose a tax year (calendar year is standard)
- File by mail or fax to the IRS

### Step 3: Set Up Payroll

Before taking any distributions, establish payroll:
- Determine reasonable salary
- Register for state employer accounts (withholding, unemployment)
- Set up payroll service (Gusto, ADP, or accountant-managed)
- Begin processing regular paychecks with tax withholding

### Step 4: Pay Yourself a Reasonable Salary

Run regular payroll (at least monthly):
- Federal income tax withholding
- Social Security tax (6.2% employee + 6.2% employer)
- Medicare tax (1.45% employee + 1.45% employer)
- State income tax withholding
- File Form 941 quarterly

### Step 5: Take Remaining Profits as Distributions

After salary, remaining profits can be distributed:
- No employment taxes on distributions
- Reported on Schedule K-1
- Subject to income tax only
- Must be proportional to ownership (S-Corp rule)

## "Reasonable Salary" — The IRS Requirement

The IRS requires S-Corp owner-employees to pay themselves a "reasonable salary" before taking distributions. Setting your salary too low triggers IRS scrutiny and potential reclassification of distributions as wages (plus penalties).

### What "Reasonable" Means

The IRS examines:
- **Comparable salaries** for similar roles in your industry and geographic area
- **Your duties and responsibilities** — what would you pay someone to do your job?
- **Time dedicated** to the business
- **Revenue and profitability** of the business
- **Salaries of other employees** (if any)
- **History** — what did you earn before S-Corp election?

### Salary Guidelines by Income Level

| Net Business Income | Suggested Salary Range | Salary % of Income |
|:------------------:|:---------------------:|:-----------------:|
| $60,000 | $35,000-$45,000 | 58-75% |
| $80,000 | $40,000-$55,000 | 50-69% |
| $100,000 | $50,000-$65,000 | 50-65% |
| $150,000 | $65,000-$90,000 | 43-60% |
| $200,000 | $80,000-$120,000 | 40-60% |
| $300,000 | $100,000-$150,000 | 33-50% |

**Lower income = higher salary percentage.** At $60,000, setting a $20,000 salary is indefensible. At $300,000, a $100,000 salary may be perfectly reasonable depending on your role and industry.

### Red Flags That Trigger IRS Attention

- Salary of $0 with large distributions
- Salary significantly below industry norms for comparable positions
- Salary that hasn't increased despite growing business revenue
- Salary inconsistent with the owner's qualifications and time commitment

## Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction Impact

Both LLCs and S-Corps can benefit from the Section 199A QBI deduction (20% deduction on qualified business income). However, the interplay with S-Corp wages matters:

- **LLC:** QBI deduction calculated on all net business income (minus 50% of SE tax)
- **S-Corp:** QBI deduction calculated on distributions only (salary is W-2 income, not QBI)
- **Net effect:** S-Corp may reduce your QBI deduction, partially offsetting SE tax savings

For income above $191,950 (single) or $383,900 (married filing jointly) in 2026, the QBI deduction phases out for "specified service trades or businesses" (consulting, law, medicine, accounting, etc.).

**Important:** Run the numbers with BOTH SE tax savings AND QBI deduction impact before making your decision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should I make the S-Corp election?

The general consensus: when your net business income consistently exceeds $60,000-$80,000/year, and the savings from reduced self-employment tax exceed the additional costs of payroll, S-Corp tax returns, and increased accounting complexity. The key word is "consistently" — one good year followed by a loss year makes S-Corp less attractive.

### Can I switch back from S-Corp to LLC tax treatment?

Yes, by revoking the S-Corp election (IRS consent required). However, once revoked, you generally cannot re-elect S-Corp status for 5 years. This is not something to flip on and off based on annual income fluctuations.

### What happens if the IRS says my salary is too low?

The IRS reclassifies some or all distributions as wages, assessing back employment taxes (employer AND employee portions) plus penalties and interest. The employer portion is not deductible. This can result in a tax bill significantly larger than what you saved.

### Do I need a new EIN for S-Corp election?

No — if you're keeping the same LLC and just electing S-Corp tax treatment, your EIN stays the same. You only need a new EIN if you create a completely new entity.

### Can a multi-member LLC elect S-Corp?

Yes — but all members must consent, and profits must be distributed proportional to ownership (no special allocations allowed, unlike a partnership-taxed LLC). This removes one of the key advantages of partnership taxation.

### Does S-Corp election affect my state taxes?

In most states, yes. Some states don't recognize S-Corp status and tax the entity as a C-Corp at the state level (New Hampshire, Tennessee for certain taxes, New York City). Check your state's treatment before electing.
