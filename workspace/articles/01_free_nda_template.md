## What Is a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)?

A Non-Disclosure Agreement — also called a confidentiality agreement, secrecy agreement, or proprietary information agreement — is a legally binding contract that establishes a confidential relationship between parties. The party (or parties) signing the NDA agree that sensitive information they may obtain will not be disclosed to unauthorized third parties.

NDAs protect everything from trade secrets and business strategies to client lists, proprietary software code, financial data, and unpublished creative works. Without an NDA, you have limited legal recourse if someone shares your confidential information — even if they verbally promised to keep it secret.

**Key Point:** Verbal confidentiality promises are extremely difficult to enforce in court. A signed NDA creates clear, documented evidence of the confidentiality obligation, the specific information covered, and the consequences of a breach.

## When Do You Need an NDA? (12 Common Scenarios)

An NDA isn't just for large corporations. Small businesses, freelancers, startups, and individuals use NDAs daily. Here are the most common situations:

### Business & Employment

1. **Hiring employees or contractors** — Before they access your internal systems, client databases, pricing models, or proprietary processes.

2. **Discussing a business partnership** — When exploring a joint venture, merger, or strategic alliance where both sides share financials and strategies.

3. **Pitching to investors** — Before revealing detailed financial projections, customer metrics, or technology specifics to potential investors or VCs.

4. **Working with vendors or suppliers** — When a vendor needs access to your systems, customer data, or operational details to provide their service.

5. **Licensing intellectual property** — Before sharing proprietary formulas, designs, source code, or manufacturing processes with a potential licensee.

### Creative & Technology

6. **Sharing a business idea** — When presenting a novel concept to potential partners, developers, or manufacturers who could replicate it.

7. **Software development** — Before hiring developers who will see your source code, architecture diagrams, or algorithmic approaches.

8. **Product prototyping** — When working with manufacturers, 3D printing services, or design firms on unreleased products.

### Personal & Professional

9. **Selling a business** — Potential buyers need to review financials, customer contracts, and operations — all highly sensitive during due diligence.

10. **Mediation or settlement discussions** — Ensuring that what's discussed in dispute resolution stays confidential.

11. **Celebrity or high-profile clients** — Personal assistants, household staff, or service providers who access private information.

12. **Medical or therapeutic practices** — Beyond HIPAA, for proprietary treatment methods or research data shared with collaborators.

**Bottom line:** If you're about to share information that would hurt your business or personal interests if it became public, you need an NDA.

## What's Included in Our Free NDA Template?

Our template covers every essential element that courts require for an enforceable NDA. Here's what each section does and why it matters:

### Section 1: Parties & Effective Date

Identifies who is bound by the agreement. This seems simple, but getting it wrong is a common mistake. The NDA should name the legal entity (the LLC or corporation), not just the individual — unless the individual is personally signing.

**Why it matters:** If you name "John Smith" but he's operating as "Smith Consulting LLC," you may not be able to enforce the NDA against the LLC — or vice versa.

### Section 2: Type of NDA (Unilateral vs. Mutual)

- **Unilateral (One-Way):** Only one party discloses confidential information. Common when hiring contractors or employees.
- **Mutual (Two-Way):** Both parties share confidential information. Standard for business partnerships, joint ventures, and M&A discussions.

**Our template includes both options** with checkboxes — simply select which applies to your situation.

### Section 3: Definition of Confidential Information

This is the most critical section. It defines exactly what information is protected. Our template includes:

- **Broad category coverage:** Trade secrets, financial data, customer information, technical data, business strategies, marketing plans, personnel information, and more.
- **Specific inclusions:** Space to list particular documents, formulas, or data sets.
- **Standard exclusions:** Information that becomes public through no fault of the receiving party, was already known, was independently developed, or was received from a third party without restriction.

**Why it matters:** Courts frequently invalidate NDAs with vague definitions. "All business information" is usually too broad. Our template strikes the balance between comprehensive and specific.

### Section 4: Obligations of the Receiving Party

Details what the receiving party must (and must not) do with confidential information:

- Not disclose to any third party without written consent
- Use only for the stated purpose (the "permitted purpose")
- Protect with the same care as their own confidential information (minimum: reasonable care)
- Limit internal access to those with a "need to know"
- Notify the disclosing party immediately upon discovering any unauthorized disclosure

### Section 5: Term & Duration

Two separate timeframes:

1. **Disclosure Period:** How long the parties will be sharing confidential information (e.g., during the business relationship, during negotiations, for 2 years).

2. **Confidentiality Period:** How long the obligation to keep information secret lasts AFTER the disclosure period ends. This is typically 2-5 years for general business information, or indefinite for trade secrets.

**State-specific note:** Some states (like California) may not enforce indefinite confidentiality periods for non-trade-secret information. Our template provides options for 1, 2, 3, 5 years, or indefinite.

### Section 6: Permitted Disclosures (Exceptions)

Situations where disclosure is legally allowed:

- Court orders or subpoenas (with notice to the disclosing party)
- Regulatory requirements
- Tax audits (with restrictions)
- Disclosure to legal counsel who agree to be bound by the same obligations

### Section 7: Return or Destruction of Materials

Upon termination or request, the receiving party must return or destroy all confidential materials and certify in writing that they have done so. This includes physical documents, digital files, and copies.

### Section 8: Remedies for Breach

- **Injunctive relief:** The right to seek an immediate court order stopping further disclosure (without needing to prove monetary damages first).
- **Monetary damages:** Compensation for actual losses caused by the breach.
- **Attorney's fees:** Whether the breaching party pays the other side's legal costs.

**Why injunctive relief matters:** Once your trade secret is public, no amount of money can make it secret again. The injunction clause lets you get a court order to stop disclosure BEFORE the damage is done, without the typical requirement of proving monetary harm first.

### Section 9: General Provisions

Standard legal "boilerplate" that matters more than people think:

- **Governing law:** Which state's laws apply to disputes
- **Dispute resolution:** Mediation, arbitration, or litigation
- **Severability:** If one provision is invalid, the rest survives
- **Entire agreement:** This NDA is the complete agreement (prevents "but you also said X" arguments)
- **Amendment:** Changes must be in writing
- **Assignment:** Whether the NDA can be transferred to successors

## State-Specific NDA Considerations (2026)

NDA enforceability varies by state. Here are key differences:

### California
- Non-compete NDAs are largely unenforceable (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code 16600)
- Cannot use NDAs to prevent reporting illegal activity
- AB 749 (2020) prohibits no-rehire provisions in settlement NDAs
- Trade secret NDAs are enforceable under CA UTSA

### New York
- Reasonable NDAs are generally enforceable
- Recent legislation limits NDAs in sexual harassment claims
- Courts apply a "reasonableness" test to scope and duration

### Texas
- Strong NDA enforcement — very business-friendly
- Must be "ancillary to an otherwise enforceable agreement"
- Requires adequate consideration (employment alone may suffice)

### Florida
- Very favorable to NDA enforcement
- Clear statute (Fla. Stat. 542.335) on trade secret protection
- Reasonable time and scope requirements

### All States (Federal)
- Defend Trade Secrets Act (2016) provides federal enforcement option
- Whistleblower protections: NDAs cannot prevent reporting securities violations to the SEC
- NLRA protections: Cannot prohibit employees from discussing wages/working conditions

## Common NDA Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Defining Confidential Information Too Broadly
"All information shared between the parties" will likely be struck down. Courts want specificity. Our template uses broad categories PLUS space for specific items — the recommended hybrid approach.

### Mistake 2: Forgetting the Exclusions
Without standard exclusions (publicly available info, prior knowledge, independent development), your NDA may be challenged as unreasonable. Our template includes all four standard exclusions.

### Mistake 3: Infinite Duration
While trade secrets can be protected indefinitely, general business information typically cannot. An NDA claiming 99-year protection for "marketing strategies" won't hold up. Match duration to the information's useful life.

### Mistake 4: No Remedy Clause
Without specifying remedies (especially injunctive relief), you're limited to proving monetary damages — which can be extremely difficult for confidential information. Our template includes both injunction and damages provisions.

### Mistake 5: Wrong Party Named
Signing as "John Smith" when the business is "Smith Enterprises LLC" creates enforcement problems. Always use the exact legal entity name.

### Mistake 6: No Consideration
In some states, an NDA needs "consideration" (something of value exchanged) to be enforceable. In employment contexts, the job itself is consideration. In other contexts, mutual promises of confidentiality (mutual NDA) or a separate payment may be needed. Our template addresses this with a recital of consideration.

### Mistake 7: Using a Generic Internet Template Without Customization
A one-size-fits-all NDA from a random website may use terms that are unenforceable in your state, miss critical protections for your industry, or include provisions you don't need (making it appear overbroad). Our template is designed for customization — fill in only what applies.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a free NDA template legally binding?

Yes. The legal validity of an NDA depends on whether it meets contract formation requirements (offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, legality), not on whether you paid for it. A properly executed free template is just as enforceable as a $1,500 attorney-drafted version — as long as the terms are reasonable and properly customized.

### Can I use the same NDA for employees and contractors?

You can use the same core template, but you should adjust key terms. Employee NDAs typically have narrower time limits (due to non-compete restrictions) and must comply with NLRA protections. Contractor NDAs can be broader because contractors aren't protected by employment law in the same way. Our template works for both — simply select the appropriate options.

### What happens if someone violates an NDA?

You can pursue legal remedies including: (1) injunctive relief (court order to stop further disclosure), (2) monetary damages for actual losses, (3) liquidated damages if specified in the NDA, and (4) attorney's fees if the NDA includes a fee-shifting provision. The practical first step is usually a cease-and-desist letter from an attorney.

### How long should an NDA last?

There's no universal answer. Industry standards: 2-3 years for general business information, 5 years for proprietary processes, and indefinite for true trade secrets (information that derives value from being secret and is subject to reasonable secrecy efforts). Our template lets you choose the appropriate duration.

### Do I need a lawyer to create an NDA?

For standard situations (hiring a freelancer, sharing a business plan with a potential partner, basic vendor relationships), a well-drafted template is sufficient. For high-stakes situations (trade secrets worth millions, international agreements, regulated industries, or as part of complex transactions like M&A), investing in attorney review is strongly recommended.

### Can I use an NDA to protect a business idea?

An NDA can protect the specific details of how your idea works, your business plans, financial projections, and market research. However, an NDA generally cannot protect a broad concept or idea itself — only the confidential details surrounding it. For idea protection before seeking a patent, an NDA is the standard approach for discussions with potential manufacturers, investors, or partners.

### What makes an NDA unenforceable?

Courts may refuse to enforce an NDA that is: (1) overly broad in scope ("all information of any kind"), (2) unreasonably long in duration, (3) signed under duress or without adequate consideration, (4) unconscionable or one-sided to an extreme degree, (5) attempting to prevent legally protected activities (whistleblowing, wage discussions), or (6) missing essential contract elements.
