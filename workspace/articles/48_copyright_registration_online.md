Copyright registration costs $35-$85 online through the US Copyright Office (USCO) and gives you critical legal advantages: the ability to sue for infringement, statutory damages up to $150,000 per work, and a public record of your ownership. Here's the complete step-by-step process.

## Do You Even Need to Register? (Copyright Basics)

**Copyright protection is automatic.** The moment you create an original work and fix it in a tangible form (write it down, record it, code it), you own the copyright. No registration required.

**So why register?**

| Without Registration | With Registration |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Copyright exists automatically | Same — plus it's on public record |
| Can't sue for infringement in federal court | CAN sue in federal court |
| Limited to actual damages (what you can prove you lost) | Eligible for statutory damages ($750-$150,000 per work) |
| Must prove ownership if challenged | Registration = prima facie evidence of ownership |
| No attorney's fees recoverable | Attorney's fees recoverable (if registered before infringement) |
| No Customs recordation | Can record with Customs to block imports |

**Bottom line:** Registration costs $35-$85 and dramatically increases your enforcement options if someone copies your work.

## What Can Be Copyrighted?

### Eligible Works
- Books, articles, blog posts, manuscripts
- Music (compositions and recordings)
- Photographs and visual art
- Software code
- Films, videos, and animations
- Architectural designs
- Choreography
- Podcasts and audio recordings
- Website content
- Course materials and curricula

### NOT Copyrightable
- Ideas, concepts, or methods (only the expression)
- Facts and data
- Titles, names, short phrases, slogans (trademark territory)
- Government works (federal — state varies)
- Works in the public domain
- Purely functional items without artistic elements

## Step-by-Step Online Copyright Registration

### Step 1: Determine Your Work Type

The USCO categorizes works into these types:
- **Literary works** — books, articles, software, websites
- **Visual arts** — paintings, photos, illustrations, graphic designs
- **Performing arts** — music, lyrics, scripts, choreography
- **Sound recordings** — recorded performances, podcasts
- **Motion pictures** — films, videos, animations
- **Single serials** — magazines, journals, newsletters

### Step 2: Create an Account at Copyright.gov

Register for a free account at the USCO's electronic Copyright Office (eCO) system.

**You'll need:**
- Email address
- Name and contact information
- Payment method (credit card, debit card, or deposit account)

### Step 3: Choose Your Application Type

| Application | Cost | For |
|------------|:----:|-----|
| **Single application** | $35 | One work by one author, not work-for-hire |
| **Standard application** | $85 | Multiple authors, work-for-hire, or multiple works |
| **Group registration** | $85 | Multiple related works (photos, articles, etc.) |

**Single application ($35)** works for most individual creators — one work, one author, not created as part of employment.

### Step 4: Complete the Online Application

**Information required:**
1. **Title of work** — The title as it appears on the work
2. **Year of completion** — When you finished creating it
3. **Date of first publication** — (If published; leave blank if unpublished)
4. **Author information** — Name, nationality, year of birth
5. **Claimant** — Who owns the copyright (usually the author)
6. **Rights and permissions contact** — Who to contact for licensing
7. **Correspondence contact** — Who the USCO should contact about the application
8. **Limitation of claim** — If your work contains previously copyrighted material
9. **Certification** — Electronic signature confirming accuracy

### Step 5: Upload Your Deposit Copy

You must submit a copy of the work being registered:

| Work Type | Deposit Requirement |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Published book | Best edition (1 or 2 copies depending on format) |
| Unpublished manuscript | Complete copy (digital upload accepted) |
| Photograph | Digital file (JPEG, TIFF, GIF, etc.) |
| Music composition | Audio file + sheet music (if available) |
| Sound recording | Audio file (MP3, WAV, FLAC, etc.) |
| Software | First 25 pages and last 25 pages of source code |
| Website | Representative pages (screenshots or PDF) |
| Video | Complete copy (digital upload for works under 450MB) |

**File size limit:** 500MB per upload. For larger files, use the Copyright Office's special shipping procedures.

### Step 6: Pay the Fee

- Single application: $35
- Standard application: $85
- Credit card, debit card, or USCO deposit account accepted
- Fee is non-refundable (even if registration is refused)

### Step 7: Track Your Application

After submission:
- Receive confirmation email with case number
- Track status in your eCO account
- Processing time: 2-8 months (standard) or 1-5 business days (special handling — $800 extra)
- Certificate of Registration mailed after approval

## Common Copyright Registration Mistakes

1. **Registering too late** — Register BEFORE or within 3 months of publication to get statutory damages and attorney's fees
2. **Wrong work type** — Selecting the wrong category delays processing
3. **Incomplete deposit** — Not uploading the full work as required
4. **Wrong author** — Work-for-hire: the employer is the "author," not the employee
5. **Not registering at all** — "I have copyright automatically" is true but insufficient for enforcement
6. **Registering individual works when group would work** — Paying $85 per photo instead of $85 for 750 photos
7. **Using a third-party service for simple registrations** — DIY is $35; a service charges $114-$300 on top

## Special Situations

### Work Made for Hire
If you created the work as an employee (within your job duties) OR under a signed work-for-hire agreement, the employer/commissioning party is the author and copyright owner.

### Co-Authored Works
All authors should be listed. Each co-author has equal rights to license the work (and must account to other authors for profits).

### Derivative Works
If your work is based on an existing copyrighted work (translation, adaptation, sequel), register the NEW material only and note the pre-existing material.

### International Protection
US copyright registration protects your work in 180+ countries through the Berne Convention. No separate registration needed in most countries.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does copyright protection last?
For works created after January 1, 1978: author's life + 70 years. For work-for-hire: 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation (whichever is shorter).

### Can I copyright my business name or logo?
Names and short phrases can't be copyrighted — they're protected by trademark. Artistic logos with sufficient creative expression can be copyrighted as visual art AND trademarked.

### Do I need to put © on my work?
No — copyright notice is optional since 1989 (when the US joined the Berne Convention). But including "© [Year] [Name]" is recommended because it eliminates an "innocent infringement" defense.

### What if someone copies my work before I register?
You still own the copyright, but your remedies are limited to actual damages (what you can prove you lost). You can't get statutory damages or attorney's fees unless you registered before the infringement (or within 3 months of publication).

### Can I register a website?
Yes — register the text, images, and code as separate works or together as a compilation. Update your registration when you significantly revise the content.
