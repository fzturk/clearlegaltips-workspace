## What Is a Commercial Lease Agreement?

A commercial lease agreement is a legally binding contract between a landlord (lessor) and a business tenant (lessee) for the rental of property used for business purposes. Unlike residential leases — which are heavily regulated by tenant-protection laws — commercial leases operate primarily under contract law, giving both parties significantly more freedom to negotiate terms.

This distinction matters enormously: residential tenants have statutory protections (habitability guarantees, eviction process requirements, security deposit limits) that commercial tenants typically do not. In a commercial lease, what's in the written agreement IS the law between the parties. If your lease doesn't address something, you likely have no protection.

**Key differences from residential leases:**

| Feature | Residential Lease | Commercial Lease |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| Tenant protections | Extensive statutory rights | Minimal — contract controls |
| Negotiability | Limited (many terms mandatory) | Highly negotiable |
| Maintenance responsibility | Landlord (habitability) | Often tenant (especially NNN) |
| Lease length | Typically 1 year | 3-10+ years common |
| Rent increases | Often capped by law | Freely negotiated |
| Security deposit | Capped in most states | No statutory cap |
| Early termination | Often tenant-favorable | Typically landlord-favorable |
| Improvements | Landlord responsibility | Tenant build-out common |

## When Do You Need a Commercial Lease? (8 Scenarios)

A commercial lease is required any time a business occupies property it doesn't own. Common scenarios:

1. **Opening a retail store** — Storefronts in shopping centers, strip malls, or standalone buildings require detailed leases addressing signage, operating hours, exclusive use, and common area maintenance.

2. **Renting office space** — From single-room suites to entire floors, office leases cover parking allocations, after-hours access, shared amenities, and often include service components (reception, cleaning).

3. **Leasing warehouse or industrial space** — Industrial leases address loading docks, ceiling height, floor load capacity, environmental compliance, and hazardous materials provisions.

4. **Starting a restaurant or food service** — Restaurant leases are among the most complex, covering grease traps, exhaust systems, health department compliance, percentage rent, and extensive build-out provisions.

5. **Medical or dental practice** — Healthcare leases include specialized provisions for medical waste, ADA compliance, patient privacy (HIPAA-compliant layout), and specialized utility requirements.

6. **Subleasing part of your space** — If you have more space than you need, commercial subleasing lets you offset rent — but your original lease must permit it.

7. **Expanding to a second location** — Growth often means negotiating a new lease while managing your existing one. Understanding lease structures helps you compare options.

8. **Renewing an expiring lease** — Renewal negotiations are your best opportunity to renegotiate terms. Understanding current market rates and lease structures gives you leverage.

## Understanding Commercial Lease Types (Critical Before Signing)

The lease type determines who pays for what. This single decision can mean tens of thousands of dollars difference annually. Our template includes all three major types — select the one that matches your negotiation.

### Gross Lease (Full-Service Lease)

**Tenant pays:** Fixed monthly rent. Period.
**Landlord pays:** Property taxes, insurance, maintenance, utilities, common area costs.

**Best for:** Tenants who want predictable expenses. Common in multi-tenant office buildings.

**The catch:** Landlords build expected operating costs into the rent, so the base rent is higher. You're paying for those costs either way — just wrapped into one number.

**Typical use:** Class A office space, co-working environments, medical office suites.

### Modified Gross Lease

**Tenant pays:** Base rent + some operating expenses (negotiated).
**Common splits:**
- Tenant pays utilities and janitorial
- Landlord pays taxes, insurance, and structural maintenance
- OR: Tenant pays pro-rata share of expenses above a "base year" amount

**Best for:** A middle ground — some cost predictability with fair expense sharing.

**The key provision:** The "expense stop" or "base year." The landlord covers operating expenses up to a specified amount or the base year actual cost. The tenant pays their pro-rata share of any increases above that threshold.

**Example:** Base year operating expenses are $8/sq ft. If expenses rise to $10/sq ft in year 3, the tenant pays the $2/sq ft increase on their proportional share.

### Triple Net Lease (NNN)

**Tenant pays:** Base rent + property taxes + insurance + maintenance (the "three nets").
**Landlord pays:** Only structural/roof repairs (sometimes not even that).

**Best for:** Tenants who want the lowest possible base rent and are comfortable managing property expenses. Common for standalone retail, industrial, and single-tenant buildings.

**Warning:** NNN leases transfer significant financial risk to the tenant. A property tax reassessment, major repair, or insurance rate increase hits you directly. Budget for 20-40% above base rent for "true" occupancy cost.

**Typical total cost breakdown (example):**
- Base rent: $20/sq ft/year
- Property taxes: $3.50/sq ft/year
- Insurance: $1.50/sq ft/year
- CAM/Maintenance: $4.00/sq ft/year
- **True occupancy cost: $29/sq ft/year**

### Percentage Lease

**Tenant pays:** Base rent + a percentage of gross sales above a "breakpoint."
**Common in:** Retail centers, shopping malls.

**Example:** $3,000/month base rent + 6% of gross sales above $600,000/year.

**Natural breakpoint calculation:** Base rent ($36,000/year) / percentage (6%) = $600,000. You only pay percentage rent if annual sales exceed $600,000.

## What's Included in Our Commercial Lease Template? (Section-by-Section)

### Section 1: Parties & Premises

Identifies the landlord, tenant, and exact premises being leased:
- Full legal names of all parties (entity names, not individuals, unless sole proprietor)
- Exact address and unit/suite number
- Square footage (rentable vs. usable — these differ!)
- Common areas included in the lease
- Parking allocation (number of spaces, reserved vs. unreserved)

**Critical distinction — Rentable vs. Usable Square Footage:**
- **Usable SF:** The actual space inside your walls that you exclusively occupy
- **Rentable SF:** Usable SF + your proportional share of common areas (lobbies, hallways, restrooms, mechanical rooms)
- **Load factor:** The multiplier (typically 1.10-1.25) that converts usable to rentable

**Example:** Your office is 2,000 usable SF. The building has a 1.15 load factor. You're billed for 2,300 rentable SF. At $25/rentable SF, you pay $57,500/year — not the $50,000 you'd expect from looking only at your actual space.

### Section 2: Lease Term

- Start date and end date
- Renewal options (number of renewals, length of each, rent during renewal)
- Notice period for renewal exercise (typically 6-12 months before expiration)
- Holdover provisions (what happens if tenant stays past the end date — usually 150-200% of rent)

**Renewal option considerations:**
- Options give TENANTS flexibility but reduce landlords' ability to re-lease
- Landlords often negotiate "fair market value" renewal rent rather than fixed increases
- Exercise your option EARLY — missing the notice deadline usually means losing the option entirely

### Section 3: Rent & Escalation

The financial heart of the lease:

- **Base rent:** Monthly amount and $/SF calculation
- **Rent commencement date:** May differ from lease start (free rent period during build-out)
- **Escalation method:**
  - Fixed increases (e.g., 3% annually)
  - CPI-based increases (tied to Consumer Price Index)
  - Fair market value adjustments (at specific intervals)
  - Step-up schedule (specified amount each year)

**Rent abatement:** Most commercial leases include 1-3 months of free rent at the beginning (a "concession"). Our template includes an abatement section.

### Section 4: Security Deposit & Personal Guarantee

- Deposit amount (typically 2-6 months' rent for new businesses; 1-2 months for established)
- Conditions for return
- Whether deposit earns interest
- Burndown provisions (deposit reduces over time if tenant pays on time)

**Personal Guarantee section:** Landlords often require business owners to personally guarantee the lease, especially for new LLCs. Our template includes:
- Full personal guarantee option
- Limited guarantee (capped at specific dollar amount or time period)
- Good-guy guarantee (personal liability ends when tenant vacates and pays through vacate date)
- Burnoff provision (guarantee expires after X years of timely payment)

### Section 5: Permitted Use & Exclusivity

- **Permitted use clause:** Defines exactly what business activities are allowed
- **Exclusive use clause:** Prevents landlord from leasing to direct competitors in the same property
- **Prohibited uses:** Activities explicitly banned (hazardous materials, adult entertainment, etc.)
- **Continuous operation clause:** Requires tenant to remain open during specified hours (common in retail)

**Why exclusivity matters:** Without an exclusive use clause, your landlord could lease the adjacent unit to your direct competitor. In a shopping center, this could devastate your business. Our template includes negotiation language for radius and category exclusivity.

### Section 6: Common Area Maintenance (CAM)

For Modified Gross and NNN leases:
- Definition of common areas
- CAM charge calculation method (pro-rata based on rentable SF)
- CAM cap (limits annual increases — NEGOTIATE THIS)
- Audit rights (tenant's right to verify landlord's CAM calculations)
- Excluded expenses (capital improvements, landlord's administrative costs over X%)

**CAM cap example:** "CAM charges shall not increase more than 5% per year over the prior year's actual charges, excluding property taxes and insurance." This protects you from sudden spikes.

### Section 7: Maintenance & Repairs

Clearly divides responsibility:

| Component | Gross Lease | NNN Lease |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Roof & structure | Landlord | Landlord (usually) |
| HVAC | Landlord | Tenant |
| Interior walls & floors | Tenant | Tenant |
| Plumbing (within unit) | Tenant | Tenant |
| Electrical (within unit) | Tenant | Tenant |
| Exterior/parking lot | Landlord | Tenant (via CAM) |
| Windows & doors | Varies | Tenant |
| Fire suppression | Landlord | Tenant (via CAM) |

**Our template includes a detailed maintenance matrix** — check boxes to assign each component.

### Section 8: Tenant Improvements (Build-Out)

- **Tenant Improvement Allowance (TI/TIA):** Dollar amount landlord contributes toward tenant's build-out (e.g., $40/SF)
- **Build-out specifications:** Who designs, who approves, who builds
- **Ownership of improvements:** Typically become landlord's property at lease end
- **Restoration clause:** Whether tenant must return space to original condition at lease end (NEGOTIATE removal of this clause)

**Typical TI allowances by property type (2026 market):**
- Office (Class A): $50-$80/SF
- Office (Class B): $25-$50/SF
- Retail: $20-$40/SF
- Industrial: $5-$15/SF
- Restaurant: Often $0 (tenant builds at own cost in exchange for lower rent)

### Section 9: Insurance Requirements

- **Tenant's insurance:** General liability (minimum $1M-$2M), property insurance on tenant's property, workers' comp
- **Landlord as additional insured:** Standard requirement
- **Waiver of subrogation:** Prevents insurance companies from suing the other party after a claim
- **Certificate of insurance:** Required annually and upon request

### Section 10: Assignment & Subletting

- Whether tenant can assign the lease or sublet (usually requires landlord consent)
- "Reasonableness" standard for consent (landlord can't unreasonably withhold)
- Profit-sharing on sublease (landlord may claim 50% of sublease profit)
- Recapture right (landlord can take back the space instead of approving assignment)

### Section 11: Default & Remedies

- **Tenant default events:** Non-payment, abandonment, bankruptcy, breach of any term
- **Cure periods:** How long tenant has to fix a breach (typically 5-10 days for rent, 30 days for other defaults)
- **Landlord remedies:** Eviction, acceleration of remaining rent, retention of security deposit
- **Landlord default:** Failure to maintain, failure to provide services, interference with business
- **Tenant remedies:** Rent abatement, self-help (fix and deduct), termination for material default

### Section 12: Destruction & Condemnation

- What happens if the building is damaged by fire, flood, or natural disaster
- Repair obligations and timelines
- Tenant's right to terminate if repair takes too long (typically 180-270 days)
- Condemnation (government taking via eminent domain) — how the award is divided

### Section 13: Surrender & Holdover

- Condition of premises at lease end
- Removal of tenant's property (fixtures, equipment, signage)
- Holdover rent rate (typically 150-200% of the last month's rent)
- Whether holdover creates a month-to-month tenancy or is a trespass

### Section 14: Miscellaneous Provisions

- Governing law and jurisdiction
- Notices (method and addresses)
- Quiet enjoyment (landlord won't interfere with tenant's business)
- Subordination, non-disturbance, and attornment (SNDA)
- Force majeure
- Signage rights
- Right of first refusal on adjacent space
- Landlord's right of entry (for inspections, showings)

## How to Fill Out a Commercial Lease (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Research Market Rates

Before filling in any numbers, know your market:
- Contact 2-3 commercial real estate brokers for comparable rental rates
- Check CoStar, LoopNet, or Crexi for asking rents in your area
- Understand whether quoted rates are Gross, Modified Gross, or NNN
- Calculate true occupancy cost (base rent + estimated operating expenses + utilities)

### Step 2: Select Your Lease Type

Choose based on your situation:
- **New business, limited capital:** Gross lease (predictable costs, higher base rent)
- **Established business, wants control:** NNN lease (lower base rent, manage your own costs)
- **Middle ground:** Modified Gross with expense stop and CAM cap

### Step 3: Fill in the Parties and Premises

- Use exact entity names (LLC, Inc., etc.)
- Confirm square footage independently (don't rely on landlord's representation)
- Specify the load factor and how rentable SF was calculated
- Document parking spaces with specificity (numbered, located, covered/uncovered)

### Step 4: Negotiate and Enter Financial Terms

Key points to negotiate:
- Free rent period (1-3 months is standard for new leases)
- TI allowance (get this in writing — verbal promises evaporate)
- Rent escalation caps (3% fixed is more predictable than CPI)
- Security deposit burndown (reward on-time payment with deposit reduction)
- Personal guarantee limitations (cap amount and/or time period)

### Step 5: Define Permitted Use Broadly

Your permitted use should be as broad as possible while remaining plausible:
- Bad: "Operation of a coffee shop"
- Better: "Operation of a food and beverage establishment, including but not limited to coffee, baked goods, prepared foods, and related retail products"
- Best: Includes future flexibility without being so broad the landlord rejects it

### Step 6: Address Build-Out and Improvements

Document everything:
- Who pays for what (TI allowance amount, tenant's additional investment)
- Approval process for construction plans
- Timeline and milestones
- What happens to improvements at lease end (avoid restoration obligations if possible)

### Step 7: Review Default Provisions Carefully

Ensure:
- Cure periods are reasonable (push for 10-15 days for rent, 30-45 days for non-monetary)
- Notice requirements protect you (written notice, adequate time)
- Landlord defaults have real consequences (rent abatement, termination right)
- No "cross-default" clauses connecting this lease to other obligations

### Step 8: Have an Attorney Review Before Signing

Unlike residential leases (which you can often handle yourself), commercial leases warrant professional review. The amounts at stake — often hundreds of thousands of dollars over the lease term — justify the $1,000-$3,000 attorney review cost.

## Common Commercial Lease Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Not Understanding Your True Occupancy Cost

A "$25/SF NNN" lease actually costs $32-$40/SF when you add taxes, insurance, CAM, and utilities. Always calculate total occupancy cost before comparing options.

### Mistake 2: Signing a Personal Guarantee Without Limitations

New business owners often sign unlimited personal guarantees without negotiation. Push for: (1) a cap on the guarantee amount, (2) a burnoff after 2-3 years of timely payment, or (3) a "good-guy" guarantee that ends when you vacate.

### Mistake 3: Narrow Permitted Use Clause

If your lease says "operation of a yoga studio" and you want to add retail merchandise sales, you may need landlord approval (which they can use as leverage for concessions). Negotiate broad permitted use upfront.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the Restoration Clause

Many leases require you to return the space to its original "shell" condition — including removing the $50,000 build-out you just installed. Negotiate removal of this clause or limit it to specific alterations.

### Mistake 5: No CAM Cap or Audit Rights

Without a CAM cap, your operating expenses can increase dramatically year over year. Without audit rights, you can't verify the landlord's calculations. Insist on both.

### Mistake 6: Missing the Renewal Notice Deadline

Most leases require 6-12 months' advance written notice to exercise a renewal option. Miss the deadline by one day, and you lose the option entirely. Calendar this immediately upon signing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long should a commercial lease be?

Standard terms vary by business type: retail (5-10 years), office (3-5 years), industrial (5-10 years), startup/new business (2-3 years with renewal options). Longer leases give you rate security but reduce flexibility. For new businesses, a shorter initial term with renewal options provides the best balance.

### What's a normal security deposit for a commercial lease?

Typically 2-6 months' rent for new businesses and 1-2 months for established tenants with good credit. There's no statutory cap like residential leases. Negotiate a burndown provision that reduces the deposit after 1-2 years of on-time payment.

### Can I negotiate a commercial lease?

Everything in a commercial lease is negotiable. Unlike residential leases (which are largely take-it-or-leave-it), commercial landlords expect negotiation. The most negotiable terms: free rent, TI allowance, escalation caps, personal guarantee scope, and CAM caps.

### What is a Letter of Intent (LOI) and do I need one?

An LOI is a non-binding summary of key terms agreed upon before drafting the full lease. It covers rent, term, TI allowance, and other major points. LOIs save time and legal costs by ensuring both parties agree on fundamentals before spending money on formal lease drafting. Most commercial transactions over $50,000/year use one.

### What happens if my business fails during the lease?

You're liable for remaining rent unless the lease has an early termination clause (negotiate one!). Options include: subletting (if permitted), assignment to another business, negotiating a lease buyout with the landlord, or — in worst case — the landlord mitigating damages by re-leasing and suing you for the difference.

### Do I need a commercial real estate broker?

A tenant's broker (tenant rep) typically costs you nothing — the landlord pays their commission from the deal proceeds. A good tenant rep knows market rates, identifies leverage points, and negotiates terms you wouldn't know to ask for. For leases over $50,000/year, a tenant rep almost always pays for itself.
