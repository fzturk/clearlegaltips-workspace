## What Is a Bill of Sale?

A Bill of Sale is a legal document that records the transfer of ownership of personal property from one party (the seller) to another party (the buyer). It serves as a receipt, a record of the transaction terms, and — most importantly — proof that the seller no longer owns the item and the buyer now does.

Think of a Bill of Sale as the "deed" for personal property. Just as a real estate deed proves you own land, a Bill of Sale proves you own a vehicle, boat, piece of equipment, or other tangible asset.

**What a Bill of Sale IS:**
- A written record of a completed sale
- Proof of transfer of ownership
- Documentation of the sale price (important for tax purposes)
- Protection against future claims by either party

**What a Bill of Sale IS NOT:**
- A title transfer (for vehicles and boats, you still need to transfer the title with your state DMV)
- A warranty (unless specifically stated)
- A financing agreement (use a Promissory Note for that)
- A contract for future sale (that's a Purchase Agreement)

## When Do You Need a Bill of Sale? (10 Common Scenarios)

### Vehicles & Watercraft

1. **Selling or buying a used car privately** — Required by most states for title transfer at the DMV. Protects the seller from liability for accidents after the sale.

2. **Selling a motorcycle, RV, or trailer** — Same as a car sale: documents the transfer and sale price for registration and tax purposes.

3. **Selling a boat or personal watercraft (jet ski)** — State registration transfers typically require a Bill of Sale showing the hull identification number (HIN).

4. **Gifting a vehicle to a family member** — Even gifts need a Bill of Sale (with $0 or "gift" as consideration) to transfer title. Many states waive sales tax on gifts between immediate family members — the Bill of Sale proves it's a gift.

### Personal Property

5. **Selling expensive equipment** — Business equipment, farm machinery, power tools, or professional instruments worth thousands need documentation.

6. **Selling furniture, electronics, or appliances** — When selling items of significant value (typically $500+), a Bill of Sale protects both parties.

7. **Selling livestock or horses** — Animal sales involving registered breeds, competition animals, or high-value livestock should always be documented.

8. **Estate sales and personal property distribution** — When an estate sells deceased person's property, Bills of Sale create a clear paper trail for the executor.

9. **Selling a firearm privately** — While federal law doesn't require documentation for private sales (in states that allow them), a Bill of Sale proves legal transfer and protects the seller.

10. **Business asset sales** — When selling a business or its equipment, individual Bills of Sale (or a comprehensive asset list) document each transfer.

## How to Fill Out a Bill of Sale (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Verify Ownership

Before creating a Bill of Sale:
- **Vehicles/boats:** Confirm the seller's name matches the title. If it doesn't, the title must be corrected first.
- **Personal property:** Confirm the seller has the right to sell (no liens, no stolen property, no co-owner disputes).

### Step 2: Identify the Property Accurately

For vehicles — the VIN is everything:
- Find the VIN on the driver's side dashboard (visible through windshield) or driver's door jamb
- Verify the VIN matches the title document
- Run a VIN check (NMVTIS, Carfax, or AutoCheck) to confirm no salvage title, flood damage, or open recalls
- Record the odometer reading AT THE TIME of sale

For boats:
- The HIN is typically on the starboard (right) side of the transom
- Record all engine serial numbers
- Note whether the trailer is included (and its VIN if so)

For personal property:
- Serial numbers, model numbers, and distinguishing marks
- Photograph the item at the time of sale (date-stamped)
- Note any included accessories, manuals, or packaging

### Step 3: Determine the Sale Terms

Agree on and document:
- **Price:** The actual amount the buyer is paying
- **Payment method:** Cash leaves no paper trail — bank transfers and certified checks are preferred for expensive items
- **AS-IS vs. Warranty:** Most private sales are AS-IS. If any warranty is offered, specify exactly what's covered and for how long
- **Existing liens:** If there's a loan on the item, document how it will be satisfied

### Step 4: Disclose Known Defects

Sellers have a legal and ethical obligation to disclose known material defects:
- Vehicles: mechanical issues, accident history, flood damage, frame damage, title brands
- Boats: hull damage, engine problems, prior sinkings, osmotic blistering
- Personal property: functional limitations, repairs needed, missing components

**Failure to disclose** can result in the buyer rescinding the sale, or a fraud lawsuit. Document everything you know — it protects you MORE than it hurts you.

### Step 5: Sign, Date, and Distribute

- **Both parties sign and date** — in ink, in person
- **Print names clearly** below signatures
- **Make copies:** Seller keeps one, buyer keeps one, and (for vehicles) one goes to the DMV with the title transfer
- **Notarize if required** — or if the transaction is high-value (notarization costs $5-$15 per signature)

### Step 6: Handle Title Transfer (Vehicles/Boats)

A Bill of Sale alone does NOT transfer title. You must also:
- **Vehicles:** Sign over the title (back of the certificate), and buyer registers within [X] days (varies by state: 10-30 days)
- **Boats:** Transfer state registration and/or USCG documentation
- **Notify your state:** Many states allow sellers to file a "release of liability" or "notice of sale" to document that you no longer own the vehicle — do this IMMEDIATELY to protect yourself from liability

## AS-IS vs. Warranty Sales: What You Need to Know

### AS-IS Sales (Most Common for Private Sales)

"AS-IS" means the buyer accepts the item in its current condition, with all faults, whether known or unknown. The seller makes no promises about the item's condition or fitness.

**When to use AS-IS:**
- Used vehicle sales between private parties
- Garage sales and estate sales
- Older items past any manufacturer warranty
- Items sold "for parts" or needing repair

**Important legal nuance:** "AS-IS" does NOT protect the seller from fraud. If you know the transmission is failing and say "it runs great," the AS-IS clause won't save you. AS-IS only covers defects the seller didn't know about or that were obvious upon inspection.

### Limited Warranty Sales

If you're offering any warranty, be specific:
- What's covered (engine and transmission only? everything? specific components?)
- Duration (30 days? 90 days? 1 year?)
- Dollar limits (repairs up to $X? full replacement?)
- Exclusions (abuse, neglect, modifications, wear items)

**Example warranty language:** "Seller warrants that the engine and transmission will function without major defect for 30 days or 1,000 miles from the date of sale, whichever comes first. This warranty covers repair or replacement of covered components only and does not cover labor, towing, or consequential damages."

## Bill of Sale vs. Other Documents

| Document | Purpose | When Needed |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| **Bill of Sale** | Records completed transfer of ownership | Every private sale |
| **Title/Certificate of Title** | Official state document proving ownership (vehicles/boats) | Required for registration |
| **Purchase Agreement** | Contract for a FUTURE sale (not yet completed) | Deposits, conditional sales |
| **Promissory Note** | Documents a loan/payment plan | Seller financing |
| **Receipt** | Acknowledges payment received | Simple cash transactions |
| **Deed** | Transfers real property (land/buildings) | Real estate only |

**Key distinction:** A Bill of Sale records a sale that HAS happened. A Purchase Agreement documents a sale that WILL happen. If money and property change hands simultaneously, use a Bill of Sale. If there's a future closing date or conditions to be met, use a Purchase Agreement.

## Common Bill of Sale Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Not Recording the Odometer Reading

Federal law (Truth in Mileage Act) requires odometer disclosure on vehicle sales. Failure to disclose — or fraudulently misrepresenting mileage — is a federal crime with penalties up to $100,000 and 3 years imprisonment. Always record the actual reading.

### Mistake 2: Listing "Gift" When It's Actually a Sale

Some buyers ask sellers to write "$1" or "gift" on the Bill of Sale to avoid sales tax. This is tax fraud. If audited, both parties face penalties plus back taxes and interest. State the actual price.

### Mistake 3: Not Identifying the Vehicle by VIN

Describing a car as "2019 Honda Civic, blue" isn't specific enough. VINs are unique to each vehicle and prevent "wrong car" disputes. Always include the full 17-character VIN verified against the title.

### Mistake 4: Seller Failing to File Notice of Sale

Many states allow (or require) sellers to notify the DMV that a vehicle was sold. Without this, if the buyer never registers the vehicle and gets parking tickets, red-light camera violations, or causes an accident, enforcement may come after YOU as the registered owner. File the notice the same day as the sale.

### Mistake 5: Not Getting a Bill of Sale When BUYING

Buyers are just as vulnerable without documentation. If the seller later claims you stole the property, or if the item turns out to be stolen, your Bill of Sale is critical evidence of a good-faith purchase.

### Mistake 6: Using a Bill of Sale Without Transferring Title

For vehicles and boats, the Bill of Sale supplements the title transfer — it doesn't replace it. Both documents are needed. The buyer must register the vehicle in their name within the state's deadline (usually 10-30 days) to complete the transfer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a Bill of Sale legally required?

It depends on your state and the type of property. Most states require a Bill of Sale for motor vehicle transfers through the DMV. For personal property, it's rarely legally required but always advisable for items worth more than a few hundred dollars. Even where not required, a Bill of Sale creates evidence that protects both parties.

### Does a Bill of Sale need to be notarized?

In most states, no. A Bill of Sale is valid with just the parties' signatures. However, some states require notarization for vehicle sales (Louisiana, Nebraska, New Hampshire, West Virginia, Montana for older vehicles, Maryland without title). Even where not required, notarization adds credibility if the document is ever challenged.

### Can I write my own Bill of Sale on a plain piece of paper?

Technically yes — any written document containing the essential elements (parties, item description, price, date, signatures) can function as a Bill of Sale. However, a comprehensive template ensures you don't miss critical elements like VIN, odometer disclosure, warranty disclaimers, or lien statements. A missing element could make the document insufficient for DMV registration or court evidence.

### What if the buyer doesn't pay in full at closing?

If the buyer is making payments over time, you need TWO documents: (1) a Bill of Sale recording the transfer, and (2) a Promissory Note documenting the payment terms. You may also want a security agreement giving you the right to repossess if payments aren't made. Consider using a lien on the title until paid in full.

### Does a Bill of Sale transfer the title?

No. For vehicles and boats, the Bill of Sale RECORDS the sale, but the title/registration must be separately transferred through the state DMV or marine agency. For personal property without titles (furniture, electronics, equipment), the Bill of Sale IS your primary proof of ownership transfer.

### What if I'm selling a vehicle with a loan still on it?

You have three options: (1) pay off the loan before selling and deliver a clean title, (2) use the buyer's payment to pay off the loan at closing (often done at the bank), or (3) have the buyer assume the loan (with lender approval — rare). Never sell a vehicle without addressing the lien. Our template includes a lien disclosure section for transparency.

### How long should I keep a Bill of Sale?

**Sellers:** Keep your copy permanently (or at least 7 years for tax purposes). It's your proof that you no longer own the item and your shield against future liability claims.
**Buyers:** Keep it as long as you own the item, plus 3 years after selling or disposing of it.
