## What Is a Sublease Agreement?

A sublease agreement is a contract between an original tenant (the "sublessor") and a new occupant (the "sublessee" or "subtenant") that allows the subtenant to live in all or part of the rental property for a specified period. The original tenant remains on the master lease with the landlord — the subtenant is essentially "renting from the renter."

**The critical legal structure:**

```
Landlord → Master Lease → Original Tenant (Sublessor)
                                    ↓
                           Sublease Agreement
                                    ↓
                          Subtenant (Sublessee)
```

**Key principle:** The original tenant remains FULLY liable to the landlord under the master lease. If the subtenant doesn't pay rent, damages the property, or violates lease terms — the landlord comes after the ORIGINAL tenant, not the subtenant. This is why a proper sublease agreement with security deposit and clear terms is essential.

## When Do You Need a Sublease Agreement?

### Common Subletting Scenarios

1. **Temporary work relocation** — You're assigned to another city for 3-6 months but want to keep your apartment for when you return.

2. **Summer break (students)** — College students who leave for summer but have a 12-month lease can sublet to cover rent during months they're away.

3. **Extended travel** — Long vacations, sabbaticals, or family care obligations that take you away for weeks or months.

4. **Relationship changes** — Moving in with a partner but your lease doesn't expire for 6 more months.

5. **Financial hardship** — Can't afford rent but can't break the lease without penalty. Subletting covers the rent while you find a more affordable situation.

6. **Renting a spare room** — You want someone to share your space and split costs (this may be a sublease or a roommate agreement depending on the arrangement).

7. **Military deployment** — Service members leaving for deployment may sublet rather than break their lease (SCRA provides additional protections).

## Before You Sublet: Landlord Consent (Step Zero)

**STOP — before doing anything else:** Check your original lease for a subletting clause.

### Three Common Lease Provisions:

**1. Subletting Prohibited:** Your lease says "Tenant shall not sublease." You generally cannot sublet without violating your lease — which is grounds for eviction.

**Options:** Ask your landlord to make an exception in writing. Some states require landlords to not unreasonably withhold consent even when the lease prohibits subletting (New York, San Francisco, others).

**2. Subletting Permitted with Landlord Consent:** Most common provision. You CAN sublet but need written landlord approval first.

**Process:** Submit a formal request with: proposed subtenant's information (name, employment, references), sublease term, and a copy of your proposed sublease agreement. The landlord typically has [14-30] days to respond.

**3. Subletting Permitted Without Consent:** Rare but exists. You can sublet freely. Still wise to notify the landlord and have a proper agreement.

**Our template includes a landlord consent section** with three options:
- Consent obtained (attach written approval)
- Consent pending (agreement contingent on obtaining consent by [DATE])
- No consent required (lease permits subletting without prior consent)

## How to Sublet Your Apartment (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Check Your Lease for Subletting Provisions
Read the master lease carefully. Look for terms like "sublease," "sublet," "assign," or "transfer."

### Step 2: Get Landlord Consent (If Required)
Submit a written request. Include: proposed subtenant's name, employment info, intended term, and proposed sublease amount. Give the landlord [14-30] days to respond.

### Step 3: Screen Your Subtenant
- Check credit (with their permission)
- Verify employment/income
- Contact references (prior landlords if possible)
- Meet in person
- Trust your instincts — you're ultimately responsible for this person

### Step 4: Fill Out the Sublease Agreement
Use our template. Be thorough — everything in writing. Attach the move-in inspection.

### Step 5: Provide a Copy of the Original Lease
Your sublessee needs to know what rules they must follow. Provide the master lease (or relevant excerpts) as an attachment.

### Step 6: Collect First Month's Rent and Security Deposit
Before handing over keys: first month's rent + security deposit. Have a witness or paper trail.

### Step 7: Conduct Move-In Inspection Together
Walk through the unit, document everything, both sign the inspection form.

### Step 8: Transfer Keys and Access Information
Provide all necessary keys, fobs, codes, and instructions (mail, trash, parking, building access, etc.).

## Common Subletting Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Subletting Without Written Landlord Consent
If your lease requires consent and you sublet without it, you're violating the lease. Your landlord can evict YOU (and your subtenant loses their housing too). Always get consent in writing BEFORE the subtenant moves in.

### Mistake 2: Not Collecting a Security Deposit
Your subtenant has no financial incentive to maintain the property without a deposit at stake. If they damage the unit, YOUR deposit from the landlord is at risk. Always collect a separate deposit from the subtenant.

### Mistake 3: No Move-In Inspection
Without documented condition at move-in, you can't prove what damage the subtenant caused versus what existed before. This protects both parties.

### Mistake 4: Not Providing the Master Lease Terms
If the subtenant violates a master lease term they didn't know about (no pets, quiet hours, parking rules), the consequences fall on YOU. Make sure they know ALL the rules.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting You're Still on the Hook
The biggest misconception about subletting: "My subtenant is paying rent now, so I'm off the hook." Wrong. If your subtenant stops paying, your landlord comes after YOU. You must then pursue the subtenant separately for reimbursement.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can my landlord refuse to let me sublet?

It depends on your lease and your state/city. If the lease prohibits subletting, most states say the landlord can refuse. However, some jurisdictions (New York City, San Francisco, some others) require landlords to not "unreasonably" withhold consent — meaning they need a legitimate reason to say no.

### Am I still responsible for rent if my subtenant doesn't pay?

Yes — absolutely. The sublease is between you and the subtenant. The master lease (between you and the landlord) still holds you fully responsible. If the subtenant doesn't pay you, you still owe the landlord. Your remedy is against the subtenant.

### Can I charge my subtenant more rent than I pay?

It depends on your jurisdiction. Many states allow it (and some sublessors profit slightly). However, some cities with rent control or subletting restrictions cap the sublease rent at the amount you actually pay. New York City, for example, limits sublease rent to the legal regulated rent (stabilized units).

### What happens if my subtenant damages the property?

First: the landlord deducts from YOUR security deposit. Then: YOU pursue the subtenant for reimbursement using THEIR security deposit. If their deposit doesn't cover the damage, you may need to sue in small claims court.

### Can my subtenant become the primary tenant?

Only if: (1) your lease ends and the landlord agrees to sign a new lease directly with the subtenant, OR (2) you formally assign (transfer) your lease to them with landlord approval. A sublease itself does NOT create a direct relationship between sublessee and landlord.

### Do I need to provide renter's insurance information to my subtenant?

You should strongly recommend (or require) that your subtenant obtain their own renter's insurance. YOUR policy likely doesn't cover the subtenant's belongings, and if they cause damage, their insurance would pay — protecting your deposit and your liability.
