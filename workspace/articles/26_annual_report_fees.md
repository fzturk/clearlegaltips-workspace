## What Is an Annual Report?

An annual report (also called annual statement, periodic report, or statement of information) is a filing with your state that confirms your business's current information:

- Business name and address
- Registered agent name and address
- Names of members/managers (LLC) or officers/directors (corporation)
- Nature of business
- State of formation

It's NOT a financial report or tax return — it's an administrative update that keeps the state's records current and confirms your business is still active.

## What Happens If You Miss the Deadline?

### Immediate Consequences
- **Late fee:** Most states charge $25-$200 for late filing
- **Loss of good standing:** Your business status changes from "Active" to "Not in Good Standing"
- **Cannot obtain Certificate of Good Standing** (needed for loans, contracts, some licenses)

### Extended Non-Filing (3-24 Months)
- **Administrative dissolution/revocation:** The state dissolves your LLC or revokes your corporation
- **Loss of business name:** Another entity could register your name
- **Loss of liability protection:** Courts may find your LLC "lapsed" and pierce the corporate veil
- **Cannot file lawsuits:** A dissolved entity typically cannot sue in state courts

### Reinstatement
Most states allow reinstatement after administrative dissolution:
- File all missed annual reports
- Pay all back fees plus late penalties
- Pay a reinstatement fee ($50-$500)
- Some states: gap in existence may not be retroactively cured

## How to File Your Annual Report

### Step 1: Know Your Deadline
Check the table above. Most states use your formation anniversary month; some use fixed dates (Florida: May 1, Georgia: April 1).

### Step 2: Gather Current Information
- Current business address
- Current registered agent information
- Current members/managers or officers/directors
- Any changes since last filing

### Step 3: File Online
Almost every state offers online filing through the Secretary of State's website. The process takes 5-15 minutes.

### Step 4: Pay the Fee
Most states accept credit/debit card or electronic check for online filings.

### Step 5: Save Confirmation
Keep the filing confirmation and receipt for your records. Your registered agent and accountant should also have copies.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What happens if I forgot to file my annual report for 2 years?

You'll need to file all missed reports, pay all back fees plus late penalties, and possibly pay a reinstatement fee if your entity has been administratively dissolved. Total cost varies by state but can range from $100-$1,000+. File as soon as possible — the longer you wait, the more expensive reinstatement becomes.

### Is an annual report the same as a tax return?

No. An annual report is an administrative filing with the Secretary of State confirming your business information. A tax return is filed with the IRS (and state tax authority) reporting your income and taxes. You need both, and they have different deadlines.

### Do I still need to file if my LLC had no income?

Yes — annual report requirements are based on your entity's existence, not its income. Even dormant LLCs must file annual reports (and pay franchise taxes in states like California). If you're not using the LLC, consider dissolving it to avoid ongoing fees.

### Can I file my annual report early?

Most states allow early filing within a window (typically 30-90 days before the due date). Some states allow filing any time during the filing year. Check your state's specific window.

### What's the difference between an annual report and a franchise tax?

An annual report updates your business information with the state. A franchise tax is a fee for the privilege of doing business as an LLC or corporation in the state. Some states combine them (California); others separate them (Delaware). You may owe both.
