What Is a Living Trust?

A living trust — formally called a revocable living trust — is a legal document that holds your assets during your lifetime and distributes them to your chosen beneficiaries after you die, without going through probate court. You create the trust, transfer your assets into it, and manage those assets as you always have. When you die, your named successor trustee distributes everything according to your instructions.

The word "living" means the trust is created while you are alive, as opposed to a testamentary trust, which is created by a will after death. The word "revocable" means you can change, amend, or completely dissolve the trust at any time as long as you are alive and competent.

Revocable vs. Irrevocable Trusts

The two main categories of living trusts operate very differently:

  • Revocable Living Trust: You retain full control. You can change beneficiaries, add or remove assets, change trustees, or revoke the trust entirely. Because you retain control, the assets are still considered part of your taxable estate and remain accessible to creditors. This is what most people mean when they say "living trust" and what this article primarily covers.
  • Irrevocable Trust: Once created, the terms generally cannot be changed without beneficiary consent and sometimes court approval. You give up control of the assets, but in exchange, those assets may be shielded from creditors and removed from your taxable estate. Irrevocable trusts are typically used for Medicaid planning, asset protection, or reducing estate taxes on large estates. They almost always require an attorney to draft.

How a Living Trust Avoids Probate

Probate is the court-supervised process of authenticating your will, paying debts, and distributing assets after death. It is public, slow (often 9–18 months), and expensive (attorney fees typically run 2–4% of the gross estate value). In states like California, a $600,000 estate can trigger over $15,000 in mandatory statutory probate fees.

Assets held in a trust bypass probate entirely because they are technically owned by the trust, not by you as an individual. When you die, your successor trustee simply follows your written instructions and distributes assets — no court involvement, no waiting periods, no public filings. This is why living trusts are especially valuable in California, Illinois, Florida, and other states with burdensome probate processes.

Anatomy of a Living Trust Document

Understanding what is actually inside a trust document helps you complete the questionnaire accurately and ensures your trust works as intended. A well-drafted living trust contains these core components:

The Trustor (Grantor or Settlor)

This is you — the person creating the trust. For a married couple, both spouses are typically co-trustors on a joint revocable trust. You retain full control of trust assets during your lifetime.

The Trustee

The trustee manages trust assets according to the trust terms. During your lifetime, you name yourself (or you and your spouse) as trustee, which is why you maintain complete control. You open accounts, sell property, and manage investments exactly as before — just under the trust's name.

The Successor Trustee

This is the person or institution (a bank or trust company) who takes over management when you become incapacitated or die. Choosing the right successor trustee is one of the most important decisions you will make. They must be trustworthy, organized, and willing to serve. Name at least one backup successor trustee in case your first choice cannot serve.

Beneficiaries

Beneficiaries receive trust assets after your death. You can name individuals, charities, or other trusts. You can also specify conditional distributions — for example, "distribute to my daughter outright if she is over 25, otherwise hold in trust until she reaches 25."

Trust Property

The trust schedule (sometimes called Schedule A or Exhibit A) lists the specific assets transferred into the trust. This is often incomplete in online-generated trusts — funding the trust properly is a separate, critical process (covered in detail below).

Distribution Instructions

These clauses define exactly how and when assets are distributed. They can be simple ("divide equally among my three children") or complex (staggered distributions, discretionary distributions for health and education, spendthrift provisions).

Powers of the Trustee

This section grants your trustee authority to invest assets, sell property, make distributions, and handle administrative tasks. Most state laws provide a baseline of trustee powers, but a comprehensive trust document spells them out explicitly to avoid ambiguity.

How to Create a Living Trust Online: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Choose the Right Trust Type for Your Situation

For most people, a revocable living trust is the correct choice. If you are married, you can create an individual trust or a joint (shared) trust. Joint trusts are simpler to manage and more common for couples with mostly shared assets. Individual trusts give each spouse more independent control and may be preferable in blended family situations or where spouses have significantly different asset bases.

If your primary goals include Medicaid planning, protecting assets from lawsuits, or reducing federal estate taxes (the 2026 estate tax exemption is approximately $13.6 million per individual), you likely need an irrevocable trust drafted by an estate planning attorney rather than an online service.

Step 2: Complete the Online Questionnaire

Every online service uses a guided questionnaire to gather your information. Prepare the following before you start: full legal names of all trustees and beneficiaries, Social Security numbers (for some platforms), addresses for all parties, a description of major assets (real estate addresses, financial account descriptions), and your chosen successor trustee's contact information.

Be specific with beneficiary designations. "My children equally" sounds clear but can create ambiguity if a child predeceases you — specify per stirpes (descendants inherit a deceased beneficiary's share) or per capita distribution to avoid disputes.

Step 3: Review Your Documents Carefully

Never sign trust documents without reading them completely. Check that every name is spelled correctly (exactly as it appears on government IDs), all asset descriptions are accurate, successor trustees are named in the correct order, and distribution instructions reflect your actual intentions. A single typo in a trustee's name can complicate administration later.

Step 4: Execute the Documents Properly

Signing requirements vary by state, but a living trust typically requires: your signature as trustor, notarization, and in some states, two witnesses. Do not skip notarization — an unnotarized trust may not be recognized by financial institutions or the court. Some states require the trust to be recorded (filed with the county recorder) to be effective for real estate transfers, though most do not.

Step 5: Fund Your Trust

This is the most critical step and the one most people either skip or do incompletely. An unfunded trust is essentially worthless — your assets will still go through probate because they never made it into the trust. Funding is covered in detail in the next section.

Funding Your Living Trust: The Critical Step Most People Get Wrong

Creating the trust document is only half the job. Funding — the process of transferring asset ownership from you individually to you as trustee of your trust — is what actually makes the trust work. Here is how to handle each major asset type correctly.

Real Estate

To transfer real estate into your trust, you must execute and record a new deed conveying the property from yourself individually to yourself as trustee. For example: "John Smith, an individual" transfers to "John Smith, Trustee of the John Smith Revocable Living Trust dated January 15, 2026."

The deed requirements vary by state. Most states use a quitclaim deed or grant deed for trust transfers. Key state-specific notes:

  • California: Use a Preliminary Change of Ownership Report (PCOR) with the deed to claim the parent-child or interspousal exclusion and avoid reassessment.
  • Florida: File a Declaration of Trust with the deed for homestead property. Florida has specific homestead exemption rules for trusts.
  • Texas: A Lady Bird deed (enhanced life estate deed) may be more efficient than a full trust transfer for some Texas homeowners.
  • New York: Pay transfer taxes and file an RP-5217 form when recording the deed.
  • Illinois: Title insurance companies require a recorded memorandum of trust (not the full trust document) when transferring real estate.

Most online trust services do not prepare the deeds for you. You will need to prepare deeds separately (some services like LegalZoom offer this as an add-on) or hire a local real estate attorney for $150–$400 per property.

Bank Accounts

Contact your bank and ask to retitle your checking, savings, and money market accounts into the trust's name. Bring your trust document (or a certificate of trust) and a government-issued ID. Most banks will require you to open a new account in the trust's name rather than simply changing the name on the existing account.

For joint accounts with right of survivorship, assess whether retitling is worth the hassle — joint accounts with a surviving spouse already pass outside probate. However, retitling is still advisable if the surviving spouse later remarries or becomes incapacitated.

POD (Payable on Death) accounts already avoid probate by naming a beneficiary. You can leave these as-is or retitle them into the trust — the right answer depends on whether you want the trust's distribution terms to control (if you have conditional distributions or minor beneficiaries, trust ownership is better).

Brokerage and Investment Accounts

Contact your brokerage directly. Most major brokerages (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard, Merrill) have established processes for trust account transfers. You will complete a form, provide a copy of the trust or certificate of trust, and the account will be retitled. Alternatively, you can designate the trust as a Transfer on Death (TOD) beneficiary without full retitling — this achieves probate avoidance but means the trust terms do not control the assets during your lifetime.

Life Insurance Policies

For life insurance, you generally have two options: (1) name the trust as beneficiary so proceeds pour into the trust at death, or (2) transfer ownership of the policy to the trust. For most people, naming the trust as beneficiary is sufficient and simpler. Transferring ownership to an irrevocable trust can remove the death benefit from your taxable estate, but this is an advanced strategy requiring professional guidance.

Retirement Accounts (401k, IRA, 403b)

Do not name your trust as the primary beneficiary of retirement accounts without consulting a tax advisor. The SECURE Act of 2019 and its 2022 successor legislation significantly changed inherited IRA rules. Naming a trust as beneficiary can compress the distribution period and accelerate income tax obligations for your beneficiaries, depending on trust terms. The general best practice is to name individuals as direct beneficiaries and name the trust only as a contingent beneficiary or for specific situations (minor children, beneficiaries with special needs, or spendthrift concerns).

Business Interests

If you own an LLC, corporation, or partnership interest, transferring these into a trust requires reviewing your operating agreement or shareholders' agreement first — many contain transfer restrictions. For single-member LLCs, you typically update the operating agreement and file an amendment with the state listing the trust as the new member. For S-corporations, certain trust types are eligible S-corporation shareholders; confirm eligibility before transferring.

Vehicles and Personal Property

Motor vehicles are rarely worth the hassle of retitling into a trust — the DMV process is cumbersome, and most vehicles depreciate quickly. Instead, use a pour-over will to capture vehicles at death. For high-value items like classic cars, boats, or aircraft, retitling makes more sense.

For personal property (jewelry, art, furniture, collectibles), you can transfer these to the trust via a Personal Property Memorandum or a general assignment of personal property — a simple signed document that transfers ownership without item-by-item listing. Your trust documents likely include a template for this.

State-Specific Requirements

Living trusts are governed by state law, and requirements vary meaningfully:

  • California: Notarization required; two witnesses optional but recommended; real estate deeds must be recorded with county recorder; PCOR form required for property transfers.
  • Florida: Two witnesses required in addition to notarization (unusual among states); homestead property has specific trust eligibility rules.
  • Texas: Notarization required; no witness requirement; Lady Bird deeds are a popular alternative to full trust funding for homestead property.
  • New York: Notarization required; no witness requirement for the trust itself; deed recording requires transfer taxes.
  • Illinois: Notarization required; use an Illinois Land Trust or record a Memorandum of Trust for real estate transfers.
  • Michigan: Notarization required; Michigan has a unique Ladybird deed statute as an alternative.
  • Arizona: No witness requirement; notarization required; community property considerations apply to married couples.
  • Washington: Notarization required; community property state — use a community property trust agreement for maximum tax benefits.
  • Georgia: Notarization required; two witnesses required for real estate deeds transferred into trust.
  • Pennsylvania: Notarization required; inheritance tax applies to trust distributions — plan accordingly.

8 Common Living Trust Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

1. Not Funding the Trust

The single most common — and most devastating — mistake. If your assets are not retitled into the trust's name, they will go through probate exactly as if the trust did not exist. Create a funding checklist and check it off asset by asset.

2. Forgetting the Pour-Over Will

A pour-over will acts as a safety net, directing any assets not already in the trust to "pour over" into it at death. Without it, assets outside the trust will either go to probate or pass by intestacy (state default rules). Every trust plan should include a pour-over will.

3. Naming Your Trust as IRA Beneficiary Without Expert Advice

As discussed above, this can have significant negative tax consequences under current law. Always consult a CPA or estate planning attorney before naming a trust as an IRA beneficiary.

4. Not Updating Beneficiary Designations on Financial Accounts

Beneficiary designations on life insurance and retirement accounts override your trust. If your ex-spouse is still listed as beneficiary on your 401k, they will receive that money regardless of what your trust says. Review all beneficiary designations annually.

5. Choosing an Unqualified Successor Trustee

Your successor trustee will have significant responsibilities: notifying beneficiaries, managing assets, filing final tax returns, and distributing property — often during an emotionally difficult time. Choose someone organized, financially responsible, and genuinely willing to take on the role. Consider naming a professional trustee (bank trust department) for large or complex estates.

6. Never Amending the Trust After Major Life Changes

Marriage, divorce, new children, deaths of beneficiaries or trustees, significant asset acquisitions, moving to a new state — all of these warrant reviewing and potentially amending your trust. A trust reflecting 2010 circumstances in 2026 may not reflect your actual wishes.

7. Improper Execution

Signing without a notary, using the wrong deed type for real estate transfers, or failing to meet state witness requirements can render the trust invalid or create gaps that require probate. Follow your state's execution requirements exactly.

8. Using a One-Size-Fits-All Template for a Complex Estate

Online services work well for straightforward situations. If you have a taxable estate (over ~$13.6 million in 2026), a blended family, a beneficiary with special needs, significant business interests, real estate in multiple states, or family members in other countries, a template-based trust is likely insufficient. Invest in an estate planning attorney.

Amending and Revoking Your Living Trust

One of the key advantages of a revocable living trust is its flexibility. You can change it at any time while you are alive and competent.

How to Amend a Trust

Minor changes (updating a beneficiary's address, changing a successor trustee) are handled via a Trust Amendment — a separate document that specifically identifies the section being changed and states the new language. The amendment must be signed and notarized with the same formality as the original trust. Most online services allow you to generate amendments for a fee.

For extensive changes, a Trust Restatement is more practical — this replaces the entire trust content while keeping the original trust date and name intact (which matters because assets are already titled to the trust). A restatement avoids having to retitle all assets into a new trust.

How to Revoke a Trust

To completely dissolve a revocable trust, you sign a Revocation of Trust document and retitle all trust assets back to your individual name. This is relatively rare but may be appropriate if you move to a state where a different estate planning approach is more advantageous, or if your personal circumstances change fundamentally.

Special Trust Types Worth Knowing About

While a standard revocable living trust covers most situations, several specialized trust structures address specific needs:

Pet Trusts

All 50 states now recognize pet trusts, which hold funds for the care of specific animals and name a caretaker to manage their welfare. Without a pet trust, you cannot legally leave money "to" your dog — animals have no legal capacity to own property. A pet trust designates a human trustee to manage funds for the animal's benefit according to your care instructions.

Spendthrift Trusts

A spendthrift clause within your trust protects a beneficiary's share from their own financial irresponsibility or their creditors. The trustee controls distributions — the beneficiary cannot demand a lump sum or pledge their interest as collateral. This is especially useful for young beneficiaries or those with addiction issues or chronic debt problems.

Special Needs Trusts (Supplemental Needs Trusts)

Leaving assets directly to a beneficiary who receives Medicaid or Supplemental Security Income (SSI) can disqualify them from those benefits. A properly structured special needs trust holds the assets without counting against benefit eligibility, allowing the beneficiary to receive trust distributions for supplemental expenses (technology, transportation, recreation) while maintaining their government benefits. These trusts require careful, specialized drafting — do not use an online template for this purpose.

Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs)

A CRT allows you to donate appreciated assets to a charitable trust, receive an income stream for life or a term of years, take a partial charitable deduction, and ultimately benefit a charity with the remaining assets. CRTs are sophisticated tax planning tools best created with an estate planning attorney and financial advisor.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a living trust avoid estate taxes?

No. A revocable living trust does not reduce federal estate taxes. Because you retain control of the assets, they remain in your taxable estate. Estate tax avoidance requires irrevocable trust structures or other advanced planning strategies. For 2026, the federal estate tax exemption is approximately $13.6 million per individual, so the vast majority of Americans will never pay estate taxes regardless.

Can I be the trustee of my own living trust?

Yes — and this is standard practice. You name yourself as trustee during your lifetime, maintaining complete control over all trust assets. You manage them exactly as you did before, just under the trust's name. Only when you become incapacitated or die does your successor trustee take over.

What happens to my living trust when I die?

Your successor trustee steps in, gathers and inventories trust assets, pays any valid debts and final expenses, files your final tax return, and distributes assets to beneficiaries according to your trust's instructions. For simple trusts, this process can be completed in weeks. No court supervision is required.

Do I need to register my living trust with the state?

In most states, no. A living trust is a private document that does not need to be filed with any government agency. The exception is real estate — you record deeds transferring real property into the trust with the county recorder, but the trust document itself stays private.

What is a certificate of trust?

A certificate of trust (also called an abstract of trust or trust certification) is a shortened document summarizing key trust terms — trustee's name and authority, trust date, and successor trustee — without revealing beneficiary information or asset details. Financial institutions and title companies often accept a certificate of trust instead of the full trust document, protecting your privacy.

Can a living trust protect assets from creditors?

A revocable living trust provides no asset protection from creditors. Because you retain control and can revoke it, the law treats the assets as still belonging to you. Creditor protection requires an irrevocable trust, often established in an asset protection-friendly state like Nevada, South Dakota, or Delaware.

What is the difference between a living trust and a living will?

These are entirely different documents. A living trust manages your assets during life and distributes them after death. A living will (also called an advance healthcare directive or healthcare declaration) documents your medical treatment preferences if you become unable to communicate. Both are important estate planning documents, but they serve completely different functions.

How long does it take to create a living trust online?

The document creation itself takes 20–45 minutes on most platforms, assuming you have your beneficiary and asset information ready. However, funding the trust — the process of retitling assets — can take weeks to months depending on the number and complexity of assets involved.

Can I create a living trust for free?

FreeWill offers a no-cost option for simple estates. Some county law libraries and nonprofit legal aid organizations also provide free trust templates. However, free templates carry higher risk of errors, lack state-specific customization, and offer no support if questions arise. For an asset as important as your estate, the $39–$249 cost of a quality online service is well worth it.

What happens if I move to a different state after creating my trust?

Trusts created in one state are generally recognized in all other states under the full faith and credit principle. However, you should review your trust when you move, particularly if you move to a community property state (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin) or from one. Real estate deeds in the new state must comply with that state's requirements. Update your trust's governing law clause to reference your new state.

90-Day Action Plan After Creating Your Trust

Creating the document is day one. Here is a realistic timeline for fully implementing your trust:

Days 1–7: Execute and Organize

  • Sign your trust with proper notarization and witnesses (if required by your state)
  • Create a secure digital copy and at least one physical copy in a fireproof location
  • Inform your successor trustee where the original document is located
  • Obtain a Certificate of Trust from your attorney or prepare one from your trust document

Days 8–30: Fund Financial Accounts

  • Schedule appointments at all banks to retitle checking and savings accounts
  • Contact your brokerage firm(s) to transfer or retitle investment accounts
  • Review all life insurance policies and update beneficiary designations if needed
  • Review all retirement account beneficiary designations (but do not name the trust without tax advice)

Days 31–60: Handle Real Estate

  • Prepare or hire an attorney to prepare deeds for all real property
  • Record deeds with the appropriate county recorder's office
  • Notify your homeowner's insurance company of the change in title (to avoid coverage gaps)
  • If you have a mortgage, check your due-on-sale clause — most lenders exempt transfers to a revocable trust under the Garn-St. Germain Act

Days 61–90: Handle Remaining Assets and Verify

  • Execute a Personal Property Assignment transferring household contents and personal property
  • Transfer business interests if applicable (review operating agreement first)
  • Create a comprehensive asset inventory listing what is in the trust and what is not
  • Sign your pour-over will to capture any assets inadvertently left out
  • Schedule an annual review date on your calendar — review your trust every year and after every major life change
